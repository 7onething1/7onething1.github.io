Captain America, Team Leader is a three-color Jeskai (white, blue, red) commander whose one line of text reads: whenever another Hero you control enters, it gains vigilance and haste until end of turn, and you put a +1/+1 counter on it and one on Captain America. Every Hero that enters is two counters and an attacker that can swing the same turn. The deck plays many Heroes, lets the counters stack, and turns that board into damage through payoffs that reward a modified creature. Below is a complete 100-card build, the curve behind it, and the plan for playing it out.

Captain America gives every hero a +1/+1 counter the moment it enters, plus one to himself, and lets it attack right away. So the plan is to play lots of heroes, watch them grow, then attack with the whole team. Below is the full 100-card deck and how to play it.

At a glance

  • Colors: Jeskai, white, blue, and red.
  • Hero target: 27 Heroes in the 99, 28 counting Captain America, most at two to four mana.
  • Power level: mid-power casual, a precon pointed toward focus rather than a cEDH list.
  • Primary win condition: a wide modified board given flying by Iron Man or double strike by War Machine.
  • Ideal Captain America turn: commander down by turn three, then aim to cast two Heroes during developed turns, each worth two counters.

At a glance

  • Colors: white, blue, and red.
  • Heroes: about 28, most of them cheap.
  • How you win: a big team of grown-up heroes that flies over or hits twice.
  • Best turn: play Captain America, then two heroes a turn, and watch the counters pile up.

One turn, start to finish

Say it is turn four and Captain America is down. You cast a two-mana Hero. It enters, gains vigilance and haste, takes a +1/+1 counter, and Captain America takes one too. Director Nick Fury has your Hero spells costing one less, so you cast a second Hero the same turn for two more counters. If Kindred Discovery is out set to Hero, each of those entries drew you a card. You attack with both new Heroes and Captain America, and Iron Man, if it is attacking, gives your modified creatures flying for the swing. Two cards spent, four counters made. That loop is the core of the deck, and the rest of the list exists to keep it running.

Your turn, step by step:

  1. Play a hero. It gets a +1/+1 counter, and Captain America gets one too.
  2. The hero can attack right away, because it comes in with haste.
  3. If you have the mana, play a second hero. That is two more counters.
  4. Attack with your whole team. Some cards let them fly or hit twice.
  5. Next turn, do it again. The board keeps growing.

The audit earns its keep on the close calls. Door of Destinies looks like a counters payoff, so it reads as core at a glance. It gains a charge counter only on a Hero you cast, so the tokens and reanimated Heroes this build makes do not grow it, and they still get its +1/+1 bonus. Its real charge-counter support is smaller than the raw Hero count suggests, which is the difference between a card that backs the whole engine and one that backs only the casting half.

Reading the audit uses four more of Snail's tests. Strength over count: Kindred Discovery reaches its payoff through many dependable routes: every Hero, Hero token, and reanimation. Door of Destinies has one route, direct casting, which is why it drops to situational. By stage: Kindred Discovery and Champions from Beyond pull weight from development onward, War Machine and She-Hulk are late finishers, and The Ozolith is recovery insurance, so a card that is quiet early keeps its slot when the deck reliably reaches its stage. From the finished list: every support count above is read off this 100 after the swaps rather than off EDHREC averages, so the Door of Destinies verdict already accounts for the token and recursion sources that do not grow it. Flexibility floor: tuning toward Heroes did not strip the answers, since the build still runs six spot removals, five mass-removal spells (four creature resets plus Dismantling Wave for artifacts and enchantments), five protection and counter cards, and seven card-draw effects, so it can respond to a changing board rather than only chain one big turn.

Dead-draw check: a card is only good if the cards it needs show up often. Kindred Discovery works off every hero, so it stays live. Door of Destinies grows only when you cast a hero as a spell, so hero tokens and reanimated heroes do not grow it, which makes it weaker than it looks.

Three ways a card goes dead

  • A Hero with no board behind it. She-Hulk gives your team trample and counters once a wide board is down. Drawn with nothing else out, she does little on her own.
  • Protection you cannot rebuild behind. Flawless Maneuver saves the board in front of you. Held with one creature out and nothing to follow, it saves almost nothing, so keep a Hero or two in hand to make it worth casting.
  • A strong card that ignores the plan. A five-mana bomb that neither adds a Hero, protects the board, nor turns counters into damage belongs in a different deck. It fails the five-job test even when its ceiling is high.

What EDHREC can and cannot tell you

These percentages are a snapshot of what other builders put in their lists. A high number means many decklists include the card, and it carries no claim about card quality, correct play, or copying a card during a game. Use inclusion to find cards worth trying and to spot the staples, then judge fit at your own table, because the number alone will not rank two cards or promise that one belongs in your build. Read the numbers as a starting map, then let the five-job test settle the close calls.

The percentages just say how many other people put a card in their deck. A big number does not mean the card is best, and it has nothing to do with copying a card during the game. Use the numbers as a hint about what to try, then decide for yourself.

More than casting: tokens, blink, and reanimation

The trigger is written on the word enters. Players read it as cast, and they skip that swap all the time. Any Hero that arrives under your control fires the counters, so casting is one route of several. A Hero token entering counts. A Hero returning from a blink counts. A Hero coming back from the graveyard counts. Winter Soldier, Reborn Avenger shows it clearly: when it attacks it returns a creature within its power from your yard, and when that creature is a Hero it enters, triggers Captain America, and Winter Soldier adds an extra counter on top. Kindred Discovery draws you a card on the same entry. A build that only counts cast Heroes tends to leave those repeated entries unused, and repeated entries can be more triggers from fewer cards.

So the construction target is Hero density plus a few ways to make Heroes enter more than once. Direct casting stays the backbone of the list. Token production and recursion are the secondary engines that let a smaller pool of Heroes fire the trigger again.

Read the card closely: it says "enters." A hero counts every time it shows up, even when you did not play it from your hand. A hero made as a token counts. A hero that comes back from the graveyard counts. Winter Soldier brings heroes back so they enter again and grow again. So the deck likes any card that makes heroes appear more than once.

Cashing the counters: the modified payoffs

A wide board of counters becomes damage through the payoffs. They share one keyword: modified. A creature with a counter on it is modified, so your countered Heroes meet these rewards at once.

Iron Man, Armored Avenger 92.1% / +85.1%. Every draw drops a counter on a creature, and when Iron Man attacks your other attacking modified creatures gain flying. A ground team gets into the air for a turn.
War Machine, Avenging Arsenal 91.1% / +83.6%. When War Machine attacks, your attacking modified creatures gain double strike. A wide countered board hitting twice can close a game quickly.
She-Hulk, Wallbreaker 88.4% / +81.3%. Your Heroes gain trample, and any Hero that gets blocked gains a counter per blocker, so chump-blocking your team tends to make it bigger.

Card draw feeds the same loop. Iron Man puts a counter down whenever you draw. Shang-Chi and the Ten Rings grows on every draw and, at ten counters, draws you five and gains five life. Professor Hulk draws you a card for each point of combat damage it deals. Kindred Discovery pairs with these, because its trigger draws a card and turns on Iron Man and Shang-Chi. One rules trap to know: Director Nick Fury, Avengers Tower, and Herald's Horn reveal a Hero and put it into your hand, which does not count as drawing, so those digs do not add a counter through Iron Man or Shang-Chi. Only the effects that use the word draw turn those counters on.

A creature with a counter is called "modified." When Iron Man attacks, your other modified creatures gain flying. War Machine gives them double strike. She-Hulk gives them trample and grows any hero that gets blocked. Drawing cards also adds counters, so the deck can get bigger every time you draw.

How the deck closes

A wide board is not the win on its own. One payoff turns it lethal. Attack with a countered team and War Machine gives them double strike, or Iron Man gives them flying, or She-Hulk gives them trample through the chump blocks. Any one of the three can turn a wide board into a game-ending swing when you have the power and a clear path, so keep a Hero or two back for the follow-up. Champions from Beyond adds a second gear: at eight or more attackers its Full Party mode pumps the team by four each. On the turn you go for it, know the job of each protection spell. Flawless Maneuver is the free save: it makes your team indestructible, which beats a wipe or targeted destruction, and it does nothing against exile, bounce, or a chump block. Akroma's Will is the finisher: with Captain America out you take both modes, so your team gains flying, vigilance, and double strike alongside indestructible and protection from every color, which pushes the damage through and shrugs off colored blockers and removal. A colorless flyer or reacher can still block. Both wear off at end of turn, so surviving a sweeper on the next turn takes countermagic or a card kept in hand. The optional power branch closes harder, with Shared Animosity paying per attacker and Cathars' Crusade snowballing the counters.

How you win: build a team of heroes with counters, then attack. War Machine lets them hit twice, Iron Man lets them fly, and She-Hulk lets them trample. Any one of those ends the game. Play a protection spell like Flawless Maneuver the same turn so nothing stops the swing.

Three winning turns

  • Wide board plus a multiplier. Six or more countered Heroes down, then War Machine or Iron Man attacks to add double strike or flying, and the swing is lethal.
  • Protected alpha after a rebuild. Rebuild off Nick Fury digs, hold Flawless Maneuver or Akroma's Will, then attack into an open board with the buff up.
  • Grind them out. Kindred Discovery and Champions from Beyond draw on every Hero, so even a slow game buries the table in card advantage and counters.

Interaction, protection, and the board-wipe question

A go-wide deck has one real fear, the sweeper that clears a board full of counters. This list answers that in three layers. First, spot removal keeps the path clear: Swords to Plowshares is the clean one-card answer, run in nine of ten lists. Second, cheap countermagic like Arcane Denial protects the engine and replaces itself. Third, and this is the piece a primer skips, Raise the Palisade is a sweeper you run yourself. It returns every creature that is not your chosen type to its owner's hand, so you pick Hero and every non-Hero on the table goes back to hand. Two caveats keep it honest: an opponent's own Heroes stay put, and your handful of non-Hero creatures bounce along with theirs. In a Hero-heavy list it still works as a strongly asymmetric sweeper.

When an opponent resolves a wipe instead, be honest about what dies: Captain America, Nick Fury, Winter Soldier, and the whole countered board go with it. The recovery is not on the battlefield. It waits in your hand and the command zone. Because you rarely empty your hand, you recast Captain America and rebuild with the cheap Heroes you held back, and Nick Fury or Winter Soldier help again once you recast or redraw them. The counters are gone, and the plan becomes a fresh curve of Heroes from hand.

The scary card: a "board wipe" kills everyone's creatures, and your counters go with them. Three answers: Swords to Plowshares removes one big threat cleanly; Arcane Denial says no to a spell and draws you a card; and Raise the Palisade is your own wipe: you pick Hero, and every creature that is not a hero goes back to its owner's hand. That mostly hits the other players. Their own heroes stay, and your few non-hero cards come back to your hand too. If someone else wipes the board, remember it kills your heroes and Captain America with them. The way back is the heroes still in your hand: play Captain America again and rebuild with cheap heroes, a little at a time.

The real rule: five jobs

Before a nonland card makes the deck, ask which of five jobs it covers: add Heroes, speed them out, refill your hand, protect the board, or clear a path. The best cards cover two of the five.

The old shorthand, "if it is not a Hero and does not pump, protect, or find Heroes, it is fighting the plan," fails on contact with a real hundred-card deck: it reads removal and ramp as suspects. Swords clears a path, Sol Ring and Nick Fury speed Heroes out, Kindred Discovery refills. A card that does none of the five is the one fighting the plan, and a card that does two of them is the one you cut last.

How to pick a card: ask whether it does one of five jobs, meaning it makes heroes, makes them cheaper, draws you cards, protects your board, or removes something in your way. A card that does none of the five should be cut, and a card that does two of them is a keep.

Opening hand and three mistakes

A keepable hand wants three or four mana sources with your colors covered and at least one Hero you can cast by turn two or three. A realistic seven-card keep is three sources, one accelerator or fixer, two early Heroes, and one draw or protection spell, and multi-role cards let a leaner hand work. A three-color deck wants all three colors reachable by turn three, so a two-source hand keeps only when its fixing and acceleration are strong. Ship hands with fewer than two sources, hands that cannot reach all three colors, or hands with no Hero castable by turn three.

Keep or mulligan

Strong keep
  • Three mana sources with your colors reachable.
  • An early Hero, castable by turn two or three.
  • A card-draw piece to refill.
  • A protection or recovery spell.
Risky keep
  • Only expensive Heroes, nothing early.
  • Several Equipment or Auras with no creatures.
  • Strong finishers with no early development.
  • Two sources with weak fixing.

Three common mistakes

  • Playing it as cast-only. Forgetting that tokens, blink, and reanimation also enter, so you leave the cheapest repeat triggers out of the deck.
  • Dumping into an open wipe. Emptying your hand when a sweeper is likely, instead of holding a Hero or two and leaning on Raise the Palisade and the rebuild.
  • Chasing off-plan power. Running a strong non-Hero that does none of the five jobs, thinning the Hero density that makes Captain America snowball.

Keep a hand with three or four mana sources and at least one hero you can play by turn three. Mistakes to avoid: only playing heroes from your hand and forgetting tokens and graveyard tricks; playing every hero right before a board wipe; and adding strong cards that are not heroes and do not help heroes.

Cap or Nick Fury: the two faces

The Avengers Assemble box ships with two full-identity commanders for the unchanged deck, Captain America and Director Nick Fury, and Captain America is the default for good reason. Both are Jeskai, so the color identity and most of the 99 stay the same. Captain America, Team Leader turns every Hero entry into counters and haste, which pushes a go-wide board that closes through the modified payoffs. Director Nick Fury leads a grindier build: his cost reduction and attack-triggered Hero digs make a deck built around card advantage and a steady stream of Heroes rather than a single explosive counter turn. Run Captain America for the aggressive Hero-and-counters plan this page describes. Move Nick Fury to the command zone when you would rather grind through card advantage, and the deck shifts from a counters theme toward a Legends and value theme. Other legendary creatures in the set can command rebuilt decks in their own color identities; Captain America, First Avenger, also Jeskai, is one such option, but it is not the commander of this unchanged shell.

Two leaders: the box lets you pick Captain America or Nick Fury. Captain America is the fast, go-wide plan in this guide. Nick Fury is a slower plan that draws more cards. Identical colors, a different feel.

What the numbers say

EDHREC data captured 2026-07-14

On the day this was written, EDHREC listed 2,526 Captain America decks at rank 925, and its top theme tags were +1/+1 counters (173), Heroes (138), Legends (78), and Aggro (43). That build count shows broad adoption. It does not say the deck is strong, that the crowd is right, or that the commander is easy to learn. The counts and percentages move as the sample grows, so the figures here are stamped with their capture date, and a later read may differ. The reason the deck reads as beginner-friendly is a short decision tree: play a Hero, take the counters, attack, repeat. Whether it is the simplest deck in the box to play against is a claim this page does not make.

On the day this was written, about 2,526 people had built Captain America, ranked number 925. That means lots of people build it, which does not make it the best. The number changes over time, so this page notes the day it was checked: 2026-07-14.
