Kang, Temporal Tyrant: The Second‑Draw Machine

The Doom box's control villain runs on one trigger, your second card each turn, and that trigger fires on every player's turn in the game, including all three of your opponents'. Every good card in the deck either helps you hit it or pays you off for hitting it.
Kang is a slow control villain. His whole deck is about drawing a second card every turn, and the clever part is that you can do it on other players' turns too. Each time you do, you drain the table.

Kang, Temporal Tyrant reads like two small abilities, and together they define the whole deck. When Kang attacks he connives, which draws a card and then discards one. And whenever you draw your second card in a turn, each opponent loses a life and you gain one. Read the second ability closely. It says each turn, which counts every turn in the game, your opponents' turns included. That one detail is the whole deck. You get to drain the table on your own turn and on all three opponents' turns, as long as you can manufacture a second draw during each of them. This is a Dimir control deck that wins by turning a steady drip of extra draws into damage, tokens, and eventually the game.

Kang has two little powers. He draws and discards when he attacks, and every time you draw your second card in a turn, you drain every opponent for one. The word that matters is "each turn." That means you can drain people on their turns too, as well as on yours, if you find a way to draw a second card on their turn.

The one trigger that matters

Your first draw each turn is the free one from your draw step, or, on someone else's turn, the first card you happen to draw that turn. The second draw is the one you have to manufacture. Connive is the cleanest way to do it, because a connive draws a card as it resolves, then discards one. A connive is an effect that draws you a card, so any connive you get on a turn where you have already drawn once flips straight into your second draw and fires every payoff at once. So the build question is simple: how many ways can you reliably draw a second card, and on how many of the four turns in a rotation.

You draw one card on your turn for free. The trick is drawing a second one. Connive is the easy way, since conniving draws a card and then makes you discard one. One connive on top of a card you already drew becomes your second draw and fires all your rewards.

The turn-cycle math

Here is the ceiling most Kang primers skip. In a four-player game there are four turns in every rotation. If you build one second draw into each of them, Kang drains three opponents four separate times before your next main phase. Instant-speed draw is what reaches the opponents' turns, because a sorcery-speed cantrip can only ever fire on your own. The engine runs on one clean second draw repeated four times a lap. You never need seven trips around your own upkeep.

Stack Gixian Puppeteer under that and every one of those drains is worth three instead of one, Kang's one life plus Puppeteer's two, so a full four-trigger rotation costs each opponent twelve life and gains you twelve. Stack Construct a Cosmic Cube under it and the same four triggers push four plan counters and four menace tokens a lap, so the seven-counter clock closes in under two rotations without you spending a single one of your own draw steps on it. The lesson for deckbuilding: instant-speed draw carries this deck, because it turns one payoff into four, so it belongs near the top of your priority list.

Cards that let you draw at instant speed, like Frantic Search, are the key, because they work on other people's turns. Reading the payoffs

EDHREC's high-synergy list for Kang is a stack of second-draw payoffs, and each one turns that trigger into a different kind of pressure.

Leader, Super-Genius 72%. The engine piece. Its replacement effect adds one extra card before each connive, so a single connive becomes a draw plus the connive, and it hands you a free combat connive every turn on top.
Construct a Cosmic Cube 67%. Every second draw makes a menacing Villain token and adds a plan counter. At the seventh counter you sacrifice it and control an opponent during their next turn, so the same steady value doubles as a hard clock.
Gixian Puppeteer 48%. Adds two to the drain, since every second draw costs an opponent an extra two life and gains you two, which is three total once you count Kang's one, rather than double. Kang plus Puppeteer is three life per trigger before you do anything else, and across a full rotation that is twelve.
M.O.D.O.K., Evil Intellect removal payoff. Whenever you draw your second card each turn, a target opponent sacrifices a nontoken creature of their choice. They pick the creature, so it is edict removal that clips their worst blocker rather than a targeted kill. Four second draws a lap is four forced sacrifices, which is how a durdle deck strips a board without spending removal spells.
Kid Loki 45%. Every second draw puts a counter on him, and any creature you have put a counter on this turn has hexproof. Read that carefully: on his own he only protects himself, and only on turns he grows. To shield the rest of the board you need other counter sources feeding the same turn.

The most popular reward cards each cash the second draw differently: Leader adds an extra card before each connive, the Cosmic Cube makes tokens and eventually steals a turn, Gixian Puppeteer drains two, M.O.D.O.K., Evil Intellect makes an opponent sacrifice a creature, and Kid Loki grows and protects himself. Note Kid Loki only protects creatures you have grown that same turn.

Off-turn enablers, the cards that make the math work

If instant-speed draw is what turns one drain into four, these are the pieces that supply it, and they are the cards a Kang list should prioritize over a fifth sorcery-speed cantrip.

Frantic Search 47%. An instant that draws two and untaps up to three lands. It costs three mana first and refunds it when three useful lands untap, so it is a mana-neutral second draw on an opponent's turn rather than a free one. The single best off-turn enabler in the shell.
Visions of Villainy 50%. An instant that draws two and costs one less with a Villain out. Cheap, repeatable-feeling, and legal to fire on anyone's turn.
Ledger Shredder 42%. Connives whenever any player casts their second spell in a turn, which happens on their turns too. If you have already drawn once that turn, its connive is your second draw and a Kang drain.
Baron Strucker, HYDRA Overlord 59%. The most-run card the old primer never mentioned. Villain spells cost one less, and once each turn a Villain entering may connive. That connive is a repeatable second-draw source, and the cost cut helps the whole Villain subtheme.
Madame Masque 47%. Connives on enter and then makes a Villain token on every second draw you hit, so she is an enabler and a payoff in one body.

These cards let you draw that key second card, and the best ones work on other players' turns: Frantic Search, Visions of Villainy, Ledger Shredder, plus Baron Strucker, who makes your villains cheaper and lets one connive each turn, and Madame Masque, who both draws and rewards.

The other half of connive, the discard

Every connive draws a card and then discards one, so a deck full of connives is quietly a discard deck too, and that is a second engine most Kang primers walk right past. The pile of cards you bin each turn is a resource, and a few EDHREC staples turn it into value or removal.

Moonstone, Harsh Mistress 42%. Whenever you discard a card you may exile it from your graveyard and play it until the end of your next turn, so the land or spell a connive threw away stays playable for a while. You still pay its cost and obey its timing, so this is extended card access rather than free recursion. She turns discarding into a filter rather than a loss.
Archfiend of Ifnir 32%. Whenever you discard a card, each creature your opponents control gets a minus-one-minus-one counter. Kang and his support connive over and over, so Archfiend turns the discard half of the engine into a slow board wipe that also cycles for value.
M.O.D.O.K. 40%. The other M.O.D.O.K., a flying lifelinker that pays three life to connive on your turn and gives every opposing creature minus-one-minus-one. He is a discard-and-connive engine on a body, feeding Moonstone and Archfiend as he chips the board down.

This is why the deckbuilding test below is wider than a pure draw count. A card like Moonstone can do more work than a fourth generic second-draw payoff, because she recovers what the engine throws away, and Archfiend converts the same discards into removal you were not paying for.

Connive draws a card and then makes you discard one, so Kang is secretly a discard deck as well. Moonstone lets you play cards you discarded for a little while, though you still pay their cost, so less is wasted. Archfiend of Ifnir shrinks every enemy creature each time you discard. M.O.D.O.K. connives over and over and weakens the board. The cards you throw away become a resource of their own.

Two lanes, and do not confuse them

The adds serve two different goals, and the guide should keep them apart. One lane makes Kang feel like Kang, the Villain flavor, and the other simply raises your win percentage with efficient Dimir cards. Some pieces do both, but a reader tuning for power should know which is which.

Make Kang feel like Kang

  • Baron Strucker and Madame Masque, Villain enablers
  • M.O.D.O.K. and M.O.D.O.K., Evil Intellect
  • Villain-token makers that crew and chump
  • The connive and Villain subtheme generally

Raise win percentage

  • Frantic Search, Visions of Villainy, instant-speed draw
  • Psychosis Crawler and Teferi, the drain multipliers
  • Black Market Connections, efficient repeatable draw
  • Counterspell, Arcane Denial, spot removal, protection

Alandra, the Cube, and Gixian Puppeteer sit in both lanes, since they are on-theme second-draw payoffs that also move you toward a real win. When you have to cut, keep the win-percentage lane full and treat the pure-flavor cards as the flexible slots.

Inclusion is not synergy

EDHREC prints two numbers per card, and they answer different questions. Inclusion is the share of Kang decks running the card. Synergy is how much more Kang runs it than the average blue-black deck that legally could. A high inclusion paired with a high synergy is a signature card, something Kang pulls toward far more than the format at large. A high inclusion with a low synergy is a staple, a card that is good in any deck of these colors. Reading only the inclusion column is how a primer ends up telling you to copy the popular pile. The synergy column is the one that shows you the deck's actual identity.

Interaction, the half that keeps you alive

A deck that wins slowly has to survive to get there, so the numbers back a wall of cheap answers. Counterspell shows up in fifty-three percent of decks, with Arcane Denial at forty-three percent and Lethal Scheme at forty-two percent as the removal, and the blue-black removal the deck can actually run, Go for the Throat, Reality Shift, Withering Torment, and Lethal Scheme, a board wipe or two like Toxic Deluge, and enough counters to protect the Cosmic Cube as it counts up. The tell is that most of your interaction wants to be instant, which is the same shape as your enablers: hold mana on other turns, and use it to answer a threat or to fire a draw for a Kang drain when nothing needs answering.

Because the deck wins slowly, you need lots of cheap answers: Counterspell, Arcane Denial, spot removal, and a board wipe. The nice part is they are instants, so on quiet turns you can spend that same held-up mana to draw a card and drain the table instead.

How Kang actually wins, and where he does not

Be honest about the power level first. Kang's own trigger is small, one life per opponent, three with Gixian Puppeteer, so the commander cannot close a game by himself and works instead as a value engine with pressure. The win comes from the finishers stacked on top of the draws. A table with a fast combo, a resilient storm turn, or repeated removal on your payoffs can beat you before the drip adds up, and losing Psychosis Crawler or the Cube to a wrath resets the clock. Out of the box he sits below the strongest precon commanders and rewards tuning more than most. The ceiling is real, the average first build is not. So the deck needs a clear answer to the question every reader asks, which is how the machine ends the game. Here are the three lines.

The drain kill. Stack the payoffs that trigger on each card drawn, the ones that fire on your first card as well as your second. Psychosis Crawler pings each opponent for one on every single card you draw, and Teferi's Ageless Insight replaces every draw except the first one in each of your draw steps with two cards. Read that carefully, because it is the sharpest interaction on the page. On your own turn Teferi doubles your cantrips. On an opponent's turn you have no draw step, so even your first draw becomes two cards, which means a single cheap cantrip is your first and second card at once and triggers Kang with no other draw effect. Teferi raises your draw count, which fuels Psychosis Crawler and reaches the second draw faster. The trigger count itself stays capped at one a turn, since Kang, Gixian Puppeteer, and the Cube each fire once on the second card of the turn, no matter how many cards follow it. Add Gixian Puppeteer and the whole table's life total falls in chunks you can count out several turns ahead. This is the line that closes without combat.

The Cube clock. Construct a Cosmic Cube is the second-draw deck's hard finish. Every second draw drops a plan counter, and the seven come from table turns rather than your own upkeeps: Kang attacks on your turn for one, then a Visions of Villainy, a Frantic Search, and one more held-up instant each add a counter across the three opponents' turns, so the Cube climbs a counter a turn and reaches seven in about two laps without you spending a main phase on it. You sacrifice the enchantment and control an opponent during their next turn. That means you steer their whole turn for them, and they stay the active player the entire time, so you decide their attacks, their spells, and where their permanents point. You control the player, not their permanents, so use it to make their decisions: attack a rival, tap out their mana, point their own removal at their own board, or decline to block. Handing you a permanent needs a separate control-transfer effect, the Cube alone does not do that. The menacing Villain tokens the Cube made along the way keep Kang swinging as the counters climb.

The flyer beatdown. Alandra, Sky Dreamer shares Kang's exact trigger and makes a 2/2 flying Drake on every second card each turn. In a deck built to hit that trigger on four turns a rotation, she assembles an evasive board that closes in the air, a creature finish the two spell-based lines cannot give you. Her second ability is the burst: whenever you draw your fifth card in a turn, Alandra and every Drake get plus X plus X until end of turn, where X is your hand size. On a heavy draw turn that turns a wide board of small flyers into lethal in one attack, and she is the clearest single card the old build was missing.

Read together, the three lines answer the power question. The drain and the flyers are your main kills, the Cube is the pressure valve that steals a turn when the drain is too slow, and none of them arrives without the enablers doing their job first. Build all three thinly rather than one thickly, because removal will take one of them and you want a second still standing.

There are three ways to win. One, the drain: Psychosis Crawler and Teferi make the table's life fall on every draw. Two, the Cosmic Cube: it counts up and lets you control an enemy's whole turn. Three, Alandra, Sky Dreamer: she makes a flying Drake each time you hit the trigger, so you can win with an army in the sky. Kang himself only drains a little, so you need these finishers to actually end the game.

A sample opening

Turn 1Land, then a one-drop or a mana rock. Nothing wants to be cast into open counters this early, so develop.
Turn 2A two-mana rock or a cheap connive source, so turn three has three or four mana available. Hold up a counter if you drew one.
Turn 3Construct a Cosmic Cube (three mana) so it starts counting, or Baron Strucker. Do not race ahead of your real mana; these plays assume a normal land drop plus one rock, with no fast mana.
Turn 4Kang (four mana), or Leader, Super-Genius. From here on, keep two mana up on opponents' turns: answer a threat, or fire an instant draw for a Kang drain when the coast is clear.
Turn 5Start the off-turn loop. Each instant-speed second draw is a drain and, with the Cube out, one plan counter. Count the triggers honestly: one Frantic Search plus one more off-turn draw is two counters this rotation, so seven is several laps away instead of turn five.

Mulligan priorities. Keep a hand with two or three lands, at least one cheap enabler or rock, and ideally one instant-speed draw so the trigger stays available on other turns. Mulligan a hand that is all lands, or all payoffs with nothing to draw the second card, since payoffs do nothing until an enabler turns on. A one-lander is a mulligan even with a perfect curve above it, because the deck wants to hold up mana every turn and cannot from a stumble. A good keep does not need Kang in it; the enablers and payoffs win the game, and the commander is support.

Three mistakes to avoid. First, dumping your hand on your own turns and leaving nothing for the opponents' turns, which throws away three quarters of the rotation. Second, attacking Kang into a board that can kill or chump him just to connive, when the connive is worth less than the commander. Third, spending an instant-speed draw on your own turn out of habit when you had already drawn once, since that same card drains the table if you hold it for an opponent's turn.

A good start: turn 1 build mana, turn 2 a mana rock, turn 3 drop the Cosmic Cube (three mana) so it starts counting, turn 4 play Kang (four mana) or Leader and keep two mana up, turn 5 begin the off-turn draws. These plays follow your real mana, no fast-mana tricks, and the Cube reaches seven counters over several turns instead of right away. Three mistakes: emptying your hand on your own turn, attacking Kang into a board that kills him, and drawing on your turn when holding it for someone else's turn would drain the table.

The real rule: five jobs

Before a nonland card makes the deck, ask which of five jobs it performs. The best cards do two of them.

The five jobs are: draw the second card, cash the second-draw trigger, turn a discard into value, protect Kang and the Cube or answer a threat, and close the game. The old shorthand, count your second draw and cut anything that does not touch it, sounds decisive and misses half the deck on contact. It treats interaction and recovery as suspects, and a Kang deck that cannot answer a threat or replay what it discards pays for that false purity. The five-job test fixes it. Frantic Search draws the second card, so it belongs. Moonstone turns a discard into value, so it belongs. Counterspell answers a threat, so it belongs. A card that does none of the five is the one fighting the plan, and a card that does two, an instant that both draws a card and answers a threat, or a Villain that both connives and drains, is the one you cut last.

Before you add a card, ask which of five jobs it does: draw the second card, reward that draw, use a discard, protect your stuff or answer a threat, or win the game. A card that does none of the five should be cut. A card that does two is a keeper.

Turning the Doom precon into a Kang deck

Here is the concrete part the primer owes you, and it starts with color. The Doom Prevails precon is Grixis, because its face commander, Doctor Doom, King of Latveria, is blue-black-red. Kang, Temporal Tyrant sticks to blue-black. The moment you move Kang to the command zone, every red card and every red mana pip in the deck becomes illegal by color identity, so those are your first and easiest cuts, no judgment call required. Pull the red removal, the red beaters, and anything with a red hybrid or activated cost, and you have already opened ten or more slots. Fill them with the blue-black enablers and payoffs from the lists above, weighted toward instant-speed draw so the trigger works on all four turns.

What stays is the deck's blue-black spine: the connive sources, the Villain support the stock deck already runs like Baron Strucker and Moonstone, the discard payoffs, and the draw. Madame Masque is not in the stock list, so she comes in as an addition rather than a retained card. What leaves, beyond the red, is the sorcery-speed filler and the pure single-draw value the second-draw test already flags. Pulling the red opens the slots. The section below is what to put back.

The Doom deck is three colors, blue, black, and red, because its main leader Doctor Doom uses red. Kang only uses blue and black, so when you make Kang the leader, every red card has to come out. Those are your easy first cuts. Put blue and black draw and connive cards in their place.

What the 99 cards are doing

A deck tech should describe the shape of the whole list. The commander is the easy part, and the ninety-nine behind it are the real work. The ratios matter more than the exact cards, so here is a working skeleton for that ninety-nine. It runs a low curve, most of the deck costs one to three, which is what lets you develop and still hold up an instant on someone else's turn.

The published ninety-nine below realizes these targets a little differently, roughly fourteen enablers, eight payoffs, three discard-and-recovery, and ten draw pieces, because the same overlap lets one card cover two rows. The point is the curve and the balance: enough cheap enablers that a second draw is available on every turn, enough interaction to reach the late game, and a land count on the low side because the spells are cheap.

A whole deck is 99 cards plus Kang. A good recipe: about 36 lands, 15 cards that help you draw the second card, 10 that reward it, 4 that use your discards, 10 answers, 3 protection cards, 6 mana rocks, and 12 more draw cards. Most cost only one to three mana, so you can play a card and still keep mana up on other turns.

Dead Draw Audit

Snail's method asks more than a card's headline job. It asks how often the card becomes useful, meaning how many cards in the ninety-nine feed its job, and whether it still earns its slot on a turn Kang is dead. Here is that audit as a fixed table: primary job, secondary synergy, support count in the deck, solo value without the commander, and a verdict. A verdict of core means build around it, supported means it earns its slot through the enabler package, situational means keep it lean and specific, and cut means it fights the plan.

The table is where a strong card gets an honest verdict. Kid Loki looks like a payoff and a protector, but his protection has almost no support in a deck with few other counter sources, and his solo floor is low, so he stays situational rather than core. Damocles Base reads situational once you read its text closely, since its villainous choice can hand you two cards and reach the trigger, which is more than a flavor pick even if the opponent controls the timing. Run every questioned card through these five fields before it earns a slot.

Five columns are the floor, and four deeper reads decide the close calls. Support strength beats support count: the same fifteen enablers feed Kid Loki's counter strongly and his hexproof barely, which is why he does not reach core. Independent routes raise a floor: Psychosis Crawler earns value through two unrelated systems, a drain on every card drawn and a body that scales with your hand, and Kid Loki has only one route, so a weak draw still leaves the Crawler doing work where Loki does none. A game-state read keeps the verdicts honest across a real game: the Crawler and the drain payoffs still tax the table when you are behind or at parity, the Cube is a pressure and finishing card, and the roughly ten interaction slots are the flexibility floor, the tools you do not cut for a sixteenth enabler no matter how much the second-draw count improves. Recount every one of these after your final swaps, since adding a payoff can lift an enabler and cutting draw can sink a retained card.

One declared exception is allowed for any genuine pet card. You may keep a card the audit would otherwise cut as an intentional flavor exception, as long as the deck says so out loud: state its weak states, its consistency cost, and the personal reason it stays, rather than dressing a thin-support card up as an engine piece.

This table grades each card five ways: what it does, what else it helps, how many other cards support it, whether it is good when Kang is gone, and a final verdict. Green core cards are the best, then supported, then situational, and red cut cards should come out. A famous card can still be a cut if nothing in the deck helps it work.

Dedicated Kang, or Kang in the Doom box

EDHREC records about eighty-one Kang decks, read 2026-07-14, so this is the niche pick of a box that only released in June 2026. A number that small this early cannot prove the community thinks Kang belongs outside Doom, and it is worth being straight about that. Wizards put Kang in the official Doom Prevails list on purpose, and Doom's own discard-and-connive package feeds Kang's trigger, so he has a real home there. The honest read comes down to ratios between the two builds. Lean the list toward instant-speed draw and blue interaction and you get the dedicated second-draw control deck described above, draining the table on every turn of the rotation. Keep Doom at the helm and Kang is still one of the better draw payoffs in the ninety-nine.

So pick by the game you want. If you like holding up counters and grinding a slow engine that taxes the table a little every turn, build Kang dedicated and load the instant-speed draw. If you want to attack and discard fast, that is more Doom's lane, and Kang still contributes from inside it.

Only about 81 people have built Kang so far, and the set is brand new, so it is too soon to say he must leave Doom's deck. Wizards put him in the Doom deck on purpose and he works there too. If you want a slow control deck that drains everyone every turn, build Kang on his own with lots of instant-speed draw. If you want to attack fast, keep Doom in charge and let Kang help.
