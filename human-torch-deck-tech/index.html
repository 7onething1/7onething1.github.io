Human Torch: The Table-Wide Burn

The Fantastic Four hothead does not pick one target, he hits everyone. Cast a noncreature spell and he flies with double strike and haste. Pay four colors on the swing and his combat damage copies to every other opponent. A small body burns the whole table.
Human Torch is a fast attacker. Cast a noncreature spell and he gains flying, double strike, and haste. When he attacks and you pay four colors, the damage he deals to one opponent also hits every other opponent. So he burns the whole table at once.

Human Torch costs {3}{R}, a 3/2 whose color identity is all four of white, blue, red, and green, earned through his attack ability. He has two abilities that turn one attacker into a table-wide threat. At the beginning of combat, if you have cast a noncreature spell this turn, he gains flying, double strike, and haste. And whenever he attacks, you may pay one red, one green, one white, and one blue, and for the rest of the turn, each time he deals combat damage to an opponent he deals that much to each other opponent. He is a Fantastic Four alt, and read on his own he is a four-color spellslinger that ends a multiplayer game from a small evasive body by copying its damage across the whole table.

Human Torch is a 3/2 that costs four mana. Cast a noncreature spell and he flies, hits twice with double strike, and can attack right away. When he attacks and you pay four colors, the damage he does to one opponent is also dealt to every other opponent. So a small creature burns everyone.

The engine, one attacker hits everyone

Human Torch stacks two effects that most decks pay a premium for. The first hands him flying, double strike, and haste on any turn you cast a noncreature spell, so he comes down and connects the same turn, hits twice, and dodges most blockers. The second copies his combat damage to each other opponent, which turns a single evasive swing into damage on the entire table. Double strike matters twice over here, since each of its two combat-damage steps triggers the copy, so a small body sprays the pod.

This makes the build a symmetric burn plan rather than a single-target beatdown. The Torch does not need to be large, since the copy and the double strike multiply whatever power he has across every opponent at once. The deck casts a cheap noncreature spell to turn on the keywords, holds four colors for the copy, and points a modest attacker at the closest life total to drain them all.

Two things make the Torch scary: he flies and hits twice, and his damage copies to every opponent. Because double strike hits twice, the copy happens twice too, so even a small attacker hurts the whole table. Cast a spell to turn on his keywords, pay four colors, and burn everyone.

The burn math, each point hits everyone

With double strike, a power of X deals X twice to the opponent he attacks, and the copy sends each of those hits to every other opponent, so each opponent takes two times X per attack. In a four-player game that is two times X to all three at once. The four-color tax

The copy costs one mana of every color except black, one red, one green, one white, and one blue, on every attack you want it. That is the price of turning one swing into table-wide damage, and it sets the same construction rule the other Fantastic Four commanders share: the mana base has to make all four colors on demand, every turn, on top of casting a noncreature spell. Fixing lands, four-color rocks, and mana creatures are the spine of the deck, since a turn without four colors is a swing that hits only one player.

The upside is that the keywords still land without the payment, so a short turn still gives you an evasive double striker that pressures one opponent. The build wants enough fixing that the copy is available on the turns the board is set, and enough ramp that paying it leaves mana for the noncreature spell that turns on the keywords. Fixing and the noncreature package are the two halves that keep the burn table-wide.

The copy needs one red, one green, one white, and one blue every attack. So the deck needs really good four-color mana. Without four colors he still flies and hits twice, but only one opponent takes the damage, so run lots of fixing.

Power and protection

Because each point of power is worth so much, cheap pumps do more here than on a normal beater. Auras and Equipment that add a few power turn the Torch into a table-wide clock, and anything granting extra combat steps or extra damage compounds with the copy. The other side is that he is a 3/2 who only gains his keywords on your combat, so he is fragile on other turns. A little protection keeps the plan alive: hexproof or a saved recast means a removal spell does not cost you the whole engine.

Small power boosts on the Torch are worth a lot, so auras and equipment are great. But he is only a 3/2, so keep a little protection like hexproof so he does not get removed before he swings.

Beyond the Dead Draw Audit: the construction audit

The Dead Draw Audit scores single cards. This construction audit scores the whole ninety-nine on the burn's inputs. The first rule is a floor on noncreature spells that turn on the keywords, the second is a four-color base that pays the copy most turns, and the third is a package of cheap power and a little protection, since power is worth triple and the Torch is fragile off-combat.

Read the noncreature count and the fixing as the two things that gate the plan, and the power and protection as the two that scale and shield it. Without a spell the keywords stop, without four colors the copy stops, without power the burn is slow, and without protection the Torch dies before it swings. Report three numbers when you tune the list, the noncreature spell count, the four-color source count, and the count of cheap power boosts. The honest measure is whether you can cast a spell, pay {R}{G}{W}{U}, and connect a boosted swing on the same turn.

Every revision should hold the noncreature floor and the fixing first, then the power package. A new noncreature spell should also draw or interact, a new mana source should add a color the copy needs, and a new pump should be cheap enough to leave mana for the copy. Protection stays light but real, since a fragile finisher that never connects wins nothing. The deck spends its slots on spells, fixing, and cheap power, with a small guard on the one body that carries the burn.

The Torch does not choose a target. Every point of his power is six damage across the table.

To build the Torch: run 30 to 36 noncreature spells, heavy four-color fixing, 8 to 10 cheap power boosts, and 5 to 7 protection cards. Cast a spell, pay four colors, and swing with a boosted Torch to burn the whole table. Keep him alive so he can connect.

The shell, a four-color Fantastic Four plan

Human Torch shares the four-color Fantastic Four base laid out on the Mister Fantastic deck tech, since both reward casting a noncreature spell each turn, so the fixing and the spell package carry over. Where Crystal pings for color count, Invisible Woman builds Walls, and The Thing doubles counters, Human Torch copies his combat damage to the whole table. Under him the shell adds cheap power auras and Equipment and a little protection on top of the shared spell and fixing base.

The interaction and the card draw carry over from any four-color spellslinger list, so the build changes the payoff toward evasion and power.
