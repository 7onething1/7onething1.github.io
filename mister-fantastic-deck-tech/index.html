Mister Fantastic: Cast Before Combat, Then Multiply the Trigger

The Fantastic Four commander draws you a card at combat when you have cast a noncreature spell that turn. The list reliably arms that cast on your own turn, and the five instant upgrades make it more active across the whole table, then it copies the triggers it sets off.

Mister Fantastic draws you a card at the beginning of combat on your turn, and only if you have cast a noncreature spell that turn. Read the rest of the deck and most of the good cards ask for a noncreature spell too, but they ask on different clocks. So the plan has two halves. Cast a cheap noncreature spell before combat on your own turn to feed the commander, and hold a few for opponents' turns to feed the pieces that reward every cast. The back half of the deck is about making that one spell do more, through extra colors, a copy, and doubled triggers.

Mister Fantastic gives you a card at combat, as long as you cast a noncreature spell first on your turn. Many other cards want a noncreature spell too, some on your turn and some on everyone's. So the deck is simple to steer: keep cheap spells in hand and cast them, and everything rewards you.

Three clocks, one beat

The word "noncreature spell" is on almost every reward, so it looks like one trigger. It is really three schedules, and knowing which is which tells you when to hold a spell and when to fire it.

Your combat, once. Mister Fantastic and Franklin Richards both check at the beginning of combat on your turn, and only if you have already cast a noncreature spell that turn. These want the spell down in your first main phase, before you move to combat.
Every turn, first spell. Valeria Richards draws off your first noncreature spell each turn, which includes opponents' turns. A cheap instant on their end step is a free card you could not get from the commander.
Every cast, no cap. Crystal deals damage on every noncreature spell you cast, with no once-a-turn limit, so a spell chain stacks the pings. Cosmic Crucible copies a noncreature spell you cast, but only once each turn, so the copy is a resource you spend on your biggest spell.

So the read is simple: cast one noncreature spell in your first main phase to arm combat, and keep cheap instants back for opponents' turns to keep Valeria and Crystal paying. Valeria makes both easy, since she takes one generic mana off every noncreature spell and draws you a card off your first cast each turn. She reduces friction and refills the card she prompts you to spend, and she cannot make a spell from nothing, so an empty hand still stalls the plan.

The rewards work on three clocks. Mister Fantastic and Franklin only pay at your combat if you cast first. Valeria pays on your very first spell of any turn, even an opponent's. Crystal pays on every single spell. So keep cheap spells in hand and cast one before combat, then more on other turns.

Three ways to build him

Mister Fantastic supports three real directions, and this guide follows the first. Decide which you want before you cut a card.

Value midrange (this list). Cheap noncreature spells, the spellcast payoffs, and the Fantastic Four bodies, grinding cards and Crystal damage. The most forgiving path, and the one every section below assumes.
Trigger-copy combo. Builds around the copy ability with untap effects and high-value triggers, pushing toward a burst or a near-loop. Higher ceiling, more fragile, wants a stronger table.
Token and creature value. Uses the copy on creature-making and enter triggers, playing wider and flatter. Fun and resilient, lower on the spellslinger reward.

You can build him three ways: steady value (this page), a combo that copies big triggers, or a wide token deck. This guide is the value version.

The multiplier subtheme

Mister Fantastic's activated ability costs one of every color plus a tap and copies a target triggered ability you control twice. Point it at Franklin's combat discover and one discover becomes three, the original plus two copies. Add Annie Joins Up, which fires every triggered ability of your legendary creatures an extra time, and the math climbs a step: Franklin triggers twice, you copy one of those twice, and you resolve four discovers off a single noncreature spell. The cost is real, all four colors and an untapped commander, so treat the copy as a payoff turn you set up, since few turns leave all four colors spare.

Cosmic Crucible copies the spell itself once a turn, a separate lever from the trigger copy, so a strong noncreature spell can be copied by the Crucible and then have its payoff triggers copied by the commander in the same turn. This is the layer that separates a plain spellslinger build from a strong one, casting one noncreature spell and then multiplying the rewards it sets off. One rule to hold: the Crucible copy is put on the stack, not cast, so the copy makes no cast triggers of its own, no Crystal, no Valeria, no Whirlwind. Point Mister Fantastic's copy at one of the original cast's triggers rather than at the Crucible trigger.

Some cards copy your rewards. Mister Fantastic's power copies a trigger twice, so one Franklin discover becomes three. Add Annie Joins Up and it becomes four. Cosmic Crucible copies the spell itself once a turn. One spell can pay you many times.

How Mister Fantastic wins

Read the copy ability precisely, because it matters mid-board. Mister Fantastic copies a target triggered ability you control, twice. He does not copy a spell, an activated ability, or a whole card. Cosmic Crucible is the piece that copies the spell itself, so keep the two apart when a turn gets busy.

Three lines close the game. First, Crystal as a burst clock, since every noncreature spell hits each opponent for its color count, and a chain of spells in one turn stacks those pings toward lethal. Second, a Franklin turn, where a copied discover casts several free spells, and each noncreature one among them pings with Crystal and draws with Whirlwind of Thought, and any creatures just enter the board, one turn that buries a table on cards and life at once. Third, the plain route, the Fantastic Four bodies swinging while opponents run low on answers.

Be honest about the ceiling. The copy ability taps Mister Fantastic, so without an untapper you copy one trigger per turn, which makes this a strong burst deck rather than a hard combo. A casual table feels it as a big value turn. A stronger table reaches the burst by holding the copy for a Franklin discover with Annie Joins Up online, the four-discover turn, then chaining the free noncreature spells into Crystal damage, since only noncreature discoveries ping. Add an untap effect and the copy repeats, though each repeat costs another R, G, W, and U, so the ceiling rises with your pod and your mana.

On the turns you can afford the activation, four colors plus an untap, one copy decision recurs. With both a Franklin discover and a Crystal ping waiting, copy the discover, since the card and board swing dwarfs a few points of damage. Copy the ping only when those points are lethal. Holding a turn for a bigger trigger is worth it only if you can protect Mister Fantastic in between.

Mister Fantastic copies a triggered ability, not a spell or a whole card. You win three ways: Crystal damage from casting lots of spells, a huge Franklin turn that makes free spells, or just attacking with the Fantastic Four. His copy taps him, so it is usually once a turn unless you untap him. When you copy, usually pick the biggest trigger, like a Franklin discover.

Protecting the engine

A spellslinger board draws board wipes, so the deck runs insurance. Clever Concealment, in 576 of the 1,102 decks, has convoke and phases out any number of your nonland permanents, so you tap your team to cast it cheap and step your board out of a wipe. Invisible Force Field gives up to four of your permanents indestructible, and it rebounds for a second free cast next upkeep only when you cast it from your hand. If Franklin discovers it and casts it from exile, rebound never sets up, so keep that copy in hand when you want the two-for-one. Both cards keep the engine on the table, and an engine deck that never gets swept is hard to race.

Your board can get wiped, so the deck runs protection. Clever Concealment hides your things for a turn. Invisible Force Field makes up to four of them indestructible, and if you cast it from your hand you get a second free cast next turn. They keep your engine safe.

The density the raw count hides. Of the 44 noncreature spells, 15 cost two or less, but only 6 are instants, and just 4 of those are cheap enough to hold up on an opponent's turn, Path to Exile, Bovine Intervention, Galvanic Iteration, and Invisible Force Field. Eleven cost five or more. So the every-turn payoffs, Valeria and Crystal, mostly fire on your own turn only, since the deck rarely has a cheap instant to spend on someone else's.

The pattern the audit exposes: the deck's real engine is cheap and independent, since Whirlwind, Valeria, and Crystal all keep working with the commander dead. The dead-draw risk is the seven-mana payoffs that only fire on your own turn. That is why the next cuts are five to seven-mana sorceries for one and two-mana instants, the exact fuel Valeria and Crystal need on an opponent's turn.

But the deck has only four cheap instants, so its every-turn payoffs mostly fire on your own turn. The big seven-mana cards can sit dead in your hand, so those are the first to cut for cheaper spells.

Three deeper reads finish the audit, past whether a card has partners.

Game state. The engine covers the middle of a game well and the edges poorly. In development it wants the cheap rocks and a two-drop, in advantage Franklin and Cosmic Crucible snowball, and at parity Whirlwind and Crystal grind. It is thin when behind, since the four cheap instants are its only real answers on an opponent's turn, and thin right after a wipe, since the payoffs that survive are enchantments and rocks while the bodies that pressure are gone. That is a real weakness a Dead Draw Audit is meant to surface, not a card-by-card verdict.
Selection compensation. The deck tolerates its eleven five-plus-mana spells only because it draws hard, Whirlwind, Valeria, West Coast Expansion, Recurring Insight, and Cut a Deal all refill. What it lacks is filtering and looting, so a clogged hand of expensive spells has no discard outlet to trade them away. That is the mechanism that makes each expensive narrow card tolerable, and its limit.
Flexibility floor. Ten removal and wrath cards plus three protection pieces are the coverage that keeps this from a one-note synergy pile. Do not cut that package to cram in more payoffs. The dead-draw fix is trading expensive spells for cheap ones, not trading answers for engine.

Keeping a hand

Judge an opener by two questions: can it make four colors, and can it cast a noncreature spell most turns. Keep these.

Ramp plus a draw piece. A rock or fetch with a card-draw spell or Valeria. It fixes colors and refills, the strongest keep.
Two or three cheap noncreature spells with lands. Enough fuel to arm the trigger every turn while you find payoffs.
A payoff plus protection. Valeria or Crystal with Lightning Greaves or a cheap answer, so the engine survives the first removal.
Ship a hand of only expensive payoffs. Five and seven-drops with no early plays cannot arm the trigger and sit dead, the classic mulligan.

Keep a hand that makes your colors and has cheap spells to cast. A hand with only big expensive cards should go back.

An opening sequence

A dependable line arms the commander the turn he lands.

  1. Fixing land, or Sol Ring into a second land.
  2. A Talisman or Arcane Signet, so two or three of your four colors are online by turn two.
  3. Valeria Richards, which takes one generic off every noncreature spell, colored pips unchanged, and draws off your first each turn.
  4. Cast Mister Fantastic, then any useful noncreature spell before you move to combat, so the combat trigger draws. The draw happens at the start of combat whether or not he attacks, so save Path to Exile for a creature that needs removing rather than spending it only to arm the trigger.
  5. Hold a cheap instant for an opponent's turn to keep Valeria and Crystal paying, then spend your Crucible copy on the biggest cast.

Turn one, a land or Sol Ring. Turn two, a Talisman so you have your colors. Turn three, Valeria to make spells cheaper. Turn four, Mister Fantastic and a cheap spell before you attack. Turn five, keep spells in hand for other turns.

Read one full round and you can see why cheap interaction matters. On your turn you cast a setup spell in the first main phase to arm Mister Fantastic and Franklin, then swing. On each opponent's turn you hold up a one or two-mana instant, and casting it draws off Valeria if it is your first that turn, pings with Crystal, and can trip Cosmic Crucible again. A sorcery normally works only during your own main phase, so a hand of cheap instants is worth more here than the same count of sorceries, and that is what separates a strong build from a slow one.

Here is a whole round. On your turn, cast a spell before you attack so Mister Fantastic draws. On other players' turns, cast a cheap instant so Valeria and Crystal still pay you. Instants work on everyone's turn, so they are better than sorceries here.

The one rule

Cast a noncreature spell before combat on your turn, and keep one back for theirs. If a card does not help you do that or reward you for it, it wants to be a spell that does.

That beat sorts the deck. A splashy creature that does nothing for the trigger is a worse pick here than a modest instant you can fire on any turn. Build so you always have a cheap noncreature spell in hand, then let Valeria, Crystal, Franklin, and the copy pieces turn that one cast into a full turn of value.

EDHREC's percentage measures how often a card shows up in submitted Mister Fantastic decks compared with other decks that could run it. Two limits worth naming. That deck count tallies submitted lists, not unique builders, since one person can keep several, so read it as decklists rather than people. And in his EDHREC Effect video Snail warns the site inflates already-popular cards and overrates pieces that shine only while your commander is out. Treat a high percentage as popularity, use it to find strong cards, then check each one against the three-clock plan yourself, and keep a few payoffs that still work on a turn Mister Fantastic is gone.
