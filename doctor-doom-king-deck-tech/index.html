Doctor Doom, King of Latveria costs {1}{U}{B}{R}, a 3/3 Grixis Villain with two abilities that share one resource, your discards. Whenever you discard one or more land cards, each opponent loses two life. And at the beginning of combat on your turn, a target Villain you control gains menace and connives, drawing a card and then discarding one, with a +1/+1 counter if that discard was a nonland. He is the face of Doom Prevails, and read on his own he is a Grixis discard-matters deck that turns a steady rhythm of land discards into a drain on the whole table. Doctor Doom is a three-mana Grixis villain. Discard a land and each opponent loses two life. Before combat, one of your Villains connives, which draws then discards. If you discard a land you drain the table, and if you discard a nonland your creature grows. The deck wins by discarding lands again and again.

Two engines, one resource

Both abilities run on discards, and they pull in slightly different directions. The land-discard drain wants you to pitch lands, two life off each opponent every time you do. The combat connive wants a Villain and offers a choice on its discard, a land for the drain or a nonland for a +1/+1 counter and a bigger attacker. The connive is your most reliable discard outlet, so Doom is partly self-contained: each combat he can discard a card, and steering that discard toward a land turns the connive into a drain as well as a filter.

The deck therefore has two build questions. How many separate ways can you discard a land within a turn, since each is a drain, and how many Villains can you field so the combat connive always has a target. A Doom list that answers both keeps the table bleeding while it develops a menacing Villain board underneath.

Doom's two powers both use discarding. Discarding a land drains the table. The combat connive lets a Villain draw and discard, and you pick a land to drain or a nonland to grow. The connive is your steady discard outlet, so Doom mostly feeds himself.

The drain math, per event not per card

Read the drain ability closely, because the wording sets the whole plan. It triggers whenever you discard one or more land cards, and each trigger is two life. That counts once per discard event that includes at least one land. A single event that pitches several lands still drains only two. A Windfall that dumps three lands at once is a single event and a single two-life drain. Three separate small discards that each pitch one land are three events and six life. So the deck wants many small, repeatable discard outlets rather than one big mass discard, and every one of them should be able to reach a land.

Doom's own combat connive is one guaranteed land-discard event a turn once you have a land to pitch, which is six table damage a turn before anything else. Add a rummaging outlet or a cycling land and each extra event is another six. The finished list is built to raise the number of separate land-discard events per turn, since that count is the drain clock. Combat damage from the menacing Villains and the rest of the Grixis suite closes from there.

The drain is two for each discard event that includes a land. So discarding one land three separate times is better than discarding three lands at once. Build lots of small ways to discard a land each turn.

Land or nonland, the connive choice

The combat connive forces a small decision every turn. Discard a land and you drain the table for two off each opponent. Discard a nonland and the Villain gets a +1/+1 counter and a larger menacing body. Early, when the board is contested, the counter and the evasion push real combat damage. Later, when the drain is the faster clock, the land discard closes the game. A good Doom pilot reads the board each combat rather than defaulting to one line.

Construction supports that choice by keeping spare lands to pitch. A deck that floods out has fuel for the drain, so a slightly higher land count and a few effects that draw extra lands turn what looks like a flood into ammunition. Cheap card draw refills the nonland side for the counter turns. The build wants both a land to pitch when the drain is the plan and a nonland to spare when the counter is.

Each combat you choose: discard a land to drain, or a nonland to make your creature bigger. Grow the creature when the board is tight, drain when you are racing. Keep some spare lands so you always have one to pitch.

Villain count, the combat trigger's fuel

The combat connive targets a Villain you control, so a turn with no Villain on the board wastes the ability. That makes Villain density a real construction number rather than a flavor note. The deck wants enough Villains that Doom almost always has a target at combat, and it wants a couple of them to make more Villains or to survive removal, so a single sweep does not switch the engine off. Doom himself is a Villain, which covers the floor, and building above that floor keeps the connive firing when he is answered.

Villains also carry the discard-matters payoffs the precon leans on, so the type does double duty. A high Villain count feeds the combat connive, supports the box's Villain-cost reducers, and gives the menace trigger a body worth pushing through. Count the Villains the way a Hero deck counts Heroes, and hold that count as a floor through every swap.

Doom's combat connive needs a Villain to target, so run plenty of Villains. Doom is one himself, but you want more so the power still works if he gets removed. Villains also help the deck's other cost-cutters, so they do several jobs.

Beyond the Dead Draw Audit: the construction audit

The Dead Draw Audit scores single cards. This construction audit scores the whole ninety-nine on Doom's two engines, the number of separate land-discard events you can create per turn and the Villain count that keeps the combat connive live. The first rule is a floor on repeatable discard outlets, the second is a Villain floor, and the third is a supply of spare lands to feed the drain.

Card advantage needs its own read. The connive and the rummaging outlets replace themselves while they drain, so much of the draw is folded into the discard package. Report two numbers when you tune the list, the repeatable land-discard outlet count and the Villain count, since those are the two engines. A build that reports a big discard total while most of it is one-shot mass discard is overstating its clock, because the per-event rule caps those at one drain apiece. The honest measure is separate land-discard events per turn times six table damage.

Every revision should hold both floors first. A new discard outlet should replace a weaker outlet or a spare slot, and it should be repeatable and able to reach a land. A new Villain should replace a nonVillain the deck can spare when the count runs thin. The slightly high land count is deliberate here, since a spare land is drain fuel waiting for an outlet.

For Doom, a spare land is drain fuel waiting for an outlet.

To build Doom: run 10 to 12 repeatable ways to discard a land, keep 18 to 22 Villains so the connive always has a target, and run a slightly high land count because lands are ammo. Big discard spells only drain once, so treat them as card draw rather than your main clock.

The shell, Doom or the full precon

Doctor Doom is the printed face of the Doom Prevails precon, so the stock ninety-nine already points at his engines, and the card-by-card precon audit lives on the Doom Prevails page. This page reads Doom as a dedicated commander rather than as one half of the box. The shift from the stock list is toward more repeatable single-card discard outlets, a higher Villain count, cycling and channel lands that double as drain fuel, and a land count a card or two above normal. The Loki copy plan, which the box also supports, pulls the other way and has its own Loki deck tech.

The interaction and the removal carry over, since a grind deck wants the same Grixis answers the precon runs. Doom and Loki share the same discard-and-Villain core and split on the payoff, Doom into a table-wide drain and Loki into a copied swarm. Build Doom toward the construction audit above, a deep bench of repeatable land-discard outlets, a Villain floor that survives a wipe, and lands read as ammunition.
