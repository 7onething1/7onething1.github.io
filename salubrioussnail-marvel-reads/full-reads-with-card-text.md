## Doom Prevails
*Grixis (U/B/R) · from my video: Snail's Guide to Drawing Less Junk - reducing dead draws through synergy (YouTube `zAtfawtYEw4`)*

### Doom, With No Dead Draws

*With just two commander engines, this is the cleanest of the four to audit for dead draws. Feed both and you almost never draw a blank, worked the way I work mine.*

A card goes dead when you draw it and the deck lacks the pieces that make it work. My fix is an audit: I look at each card and ask two questions, what does this card accomplish, and what else does it synergize with. Run that pass and the sluggish, swingy feeling of a deck that keeps drawing blanks starts to clear up. The Doom precon is the cleanest test case in the whole box, because its two commanders give it two primary engines, and almost every good upgrade is about replacing a card that feeds neither of them.

#### Two questions, two engines  
*The core idea*

My audit turns on task and synergy. A card that does one useful thing and links up with a package of others earns its slot, and a card that does a random thing with nothing to support it draws dead. The Doom deck makes both questions easy to answer, because it wants two things above all, and you can hold every card up against them.
- **Engine 1: the discard drain:** Doctor Doom drains the table for two every time you pitch a land, so cards that let you throw away lands for value feed him directly.
- **Engine 2: the Loki copies:** Loki makes a tapped attacking copy of another Villain each combat, and that copy comes in nonlegendary, so even your legends are fair game. Villains that connect draw you cards, so any body worth copying feeds him.

> Hold each card up to both engines. If it feeds neither the discard drain nor the copy loop, that is your dead draw, and that is the slot an upgrade should take.

This is the same move I make when I sort a deck into categories and check whether each part has enough support. Here the categories are already drawn for you by the two commanders, which is why this precon is the cleanest fit of the four for this audit. The three swaps below are that category audit carried out card by card.

#### A low-payoff card for a body worth copying  
*Swap 1*


**Superior Foes of Spider-Man → Green Goblin, Revenant**
Superior Foes of Spider-Man is a low-payoff card with no home in either engine, so it draws dead in most hands. Green Goblin, Revenant is a flying deathtouch Villain with evasion and built-in card advantage. It gives Loki a body worth copying and it connects to draw, so it belongs squarely inside the copy engine instead of drawing a blank.

#### An off-plan body for a copy-loop payoff  
*Swap 2*


**Molecule Man → Kingpin, Wilson Fisk**
Molecule Man is a six-mana 5/5 whose miracle-granting ability wants a very different deck, so it does nothing for the discard or copy engines here. Kingpin, Wilson Fisk turns your sacrificed Loki tokens into Treasure, so the copies you were making and losing at end of turn now leave value behind. A card that used to be dead becomes an active part of the copy loop.

#### An off-theme vehicle for a copy-keeper  
*Swap 3*


**Damocles Base, Sword of Kang → Sundial of the Infinite**
Damocles Base is an off-theme vehicle with no place in either engine. Sundial of the Infinite ends your turn before the delayed sacrifice resolves, so Loki's copies stick around as permanent Villains instead of vanishing at end of turn. That is the difference between a filler slot and a piece that deepens the exact engine the deck is built on.

#### Nothing generically weak comes out  
*The through-line*

Taken as a group, the swaps are my audit in action. Nothing generically weak leaves the deck. What leaves is whatever the discard drain and the copy loop cannot use, and what comes in feeds one engine or the other. Superior Foes becomes a body Loki wants to copy, Molecule Man becomes a payoff for the tokens Loki sacrifices, and an off-theme vehicle becomes a way to keep those copies for good. That is why Doom is the cleanest fit of the four for this audit. Its two commanders draw the category lines for you, so the dead-draw audit almost runs itself, and the deck stops drawing blanks.

> Feed both engines and the deck almost never draws a card that does nothing. That is the whole promise of the audit, delivered on the cleanest deck in the box.

## The Fantastic Four
*Four-color (W/U/R/G) · from my video: My Brother and I Built a Tool to Yell at You About Your Manabase - mana fixing and color consistency (YouTube `YcSHb11AqL4`)*

### The Fantastic Four's Manabase

*The only four-color deck in the box has one real tax, and it is color. Here is the whole mana base, worked the way I work mine.*

A manabase is one of the most deceptively complicated things you build, mostly because it stays invisible. Nobody describes the Fantastic Four precon by its thirty-six lands and its tapped-land rate. They describe it by Mister Fantastic copying his own triggers. The lands hold all of that up as the foundation, and a shaky foundation gives you a shaky deck. This precon feels the weight more than any of its three siblings, because it is the one deck in the set that asks for four colors of mana.

#### Two jobs, one set of lands  
*Start here*

It helps to think of a land base as covering two different goals at once. The first goal is covering your symbols. Lands exist to make the colors of mana you need to cast your spells. The second goal is covering total mana values, which mostly means hitting your land drops turn after turn so the deck keeps climbing its curve. Utility lands live in the gap between those two goals. If a deck wants thirty-six lands to reliably hit its drops but only needs thirty-three to make an acceptable spread of colors, that leaves a few slots for colorless-producing utility lands.
For the Fantastic Four, that second goal is the easy one. It runs the standard high land count a four-color midrange deck wants, and hitting drops is rarely the thing that loses the game. The first goal is where the whole deck lives or dies, because four colors is a lot of colors, and this deck wants them early and often.

#### Which colors, and when  
*Symbols and timing*

Covering symbols depends on three things at once: how many of each color you run, how intense each requirement is, and how early the deck needs it. A single colored pip you can pay late in the game asks very little of your mana. Two of one color demanded on turn three asks a great deal. Timing matters just as much as raw count, the same way a reactive spell you hold for later strains a manabase less than a threat you want to slam on curve.

> Mister Fantastic's copy ability costs {R}{G}{W}{U}. One of every color, on the same turn, for the strongest button in the deck.

Read the four faces in that light and the demand curve is clear. Mister Fantastic at {2}{U} and Invisible Woman at {2}{W} are your early asks, so blue and white need to show up first. Human Torch at {3}{R} comes a turn later. The Thing at {5}{G} is your latest ask, so a stray green source turning up on turn five is fine, the way a six-drop commander can wait. The sharpest requirement belongs to Mister Fantastic's activated ability, {R}{G}{W}{U}, which wants one of every color at once. Build the mana so that button is castable, and the rest of the deck follows.

#### Is the trouble fixing, or too few lands?  
*The diagnosis*

I built a free manabase tool with my brother Ian that scores a deck two ways: cast rate, meaning how often a card is castable on or before its mana value, and average delay, meaning how many turns you spend waiting on the right lands. The clever part is the test it runs on top of that. It tries adding one basic of each color, and it tries adding a single colorless "waste." If the waste alone drops your delay a lot, the deck is short on lands. If a colored basic helps far more than the waste, the deck is short on that color, and land count is fine.
Count the sources first. Across the thirty-six lands plus Arcane Signet and Chromatic Lantern, and counting basic-fetchers and any-color pieces toward each color, this precon runs sixteen white sources, sixteen blue, fourteen red, and seventeen green. No single color is starved, so the failure is neither land count nor a missing color. The tax on a four-color deck is hitting two or three colors at once on the early turns, and cheap untapped fixing is what smooths that. That is exactly what the two Talismans add, two two-mana rocks that each cover two colors and never enter tapped. For the precise cast-rate and average-delay figures, run the list through my manabase tool; the counts here are what the swaps move.

#### A cute legend for a fixing rock  
*Swap 1*


**Willie Lumpkin, Postman → Talisman of Creativity**
Willie is flavor. He is a body that does nothing for the goal that actually gates this deck, which is making four colors on time. Talisman of Creativity taps for blue or red on turn two, a turn ahead of your three-mana commander, so you have two of your four colors covered when Mister Fantastic comes down on turn three. A fixing rock on curve does far more for a build this greedy than a charming legend ever could.

#### Two Talismans is the deck confessing  
*Swap 2*


**Mirage Mirror → Talisman of Conviction**
Mirage Mirror is a slow, fiddly toolbox that gives back very little in a deck with this much else going on. Talisman of Conviction taps for white or red, and the deck wants it. Two Talismans in the ninety-nine is the deck telling you plainly where its trouble really is. Between them, Creativity and Conviction reach three of your four colors and double up on red, which smooths the exact hands where the four-color draw was going to stall.

#### The third swap upgrades your draw  
*Swap 3*


**Into the Time Vortex → West Coast Expansion**
The two Talismans carry the color fixing. This third swap is about card flow. Into the Time Vortex is a cascade and rebound sorcery whose value depends on what you hit off the top, so it runs feast or famine. West Coast Expansion draws X cards, and when X reaches five it lets you cast a Hero for free, a cleaner refill for a deck that wants a noncreature spell every turn. If you would rather spend this slot on color, a Fellwar Stone or a third Talisman is the fixing pick.

#### Tapped lands cost you the turns you care about  
*The budget trap*

Every budget four-color deck relies on dual lands that enter tapped, and the tool is blunt about the cost. A land that comes in tapped is a turn you did not get to act. That matters more here than in most decks, because the Fantastic Four wants a noncreature spell down every single turn to keep all four faces awake. A hand full of tapped duals on turns two and three is a hand that missed two of its most important windows. The number to watch is how often each land actually enters untapped. Conditional lands vary wildly, from a battle land that needs two basics to a slow land that needs two other lands already down, so check that the cheap duals you add are really coming in untapped often enough to earn the slot.

#### How safe do you want to feel  
*Your call*

I am careful not to hand you a single correct land count, because the right number depends on how much risk you enjoy. A lower land count means more action and more games where the mana lets you down. A higher count means fewer explosive draws and far fewer games lost to a missing color. The Fantastic Four belongs on the safe end of that choice for most tables. Its ceiling comes from Mister Fantastic once the four colors are online, and you only get there by making sure they arrive on time. Both goals of the land base, symbols and land drops, point the same direction for this deck: pay the fixing tax in full, and the ceiling takes care of itself.

> The four-color mana is the tax you pay for the highest ceiling of the four by the published rankings. Pay it in color, and the deck stops stumbling on the turns it wants to break open.

## Avengers Assemble
*Jeskai (R/W/U) · from my video: The Case for Simpler Decks in Commander (YouTube `PFdpbJPArU4`)*

### The Avengers, Kept Legible

*The easiest of the four to pilot wins by being easy to read. Here is why that matters, and how to upgrade it without clouding the board, worked the way I work mine.*

Sit down to a pickup game of Commander with three strangers and three decks you have never seen, and the hardest thing to handle is the sheer weight of information, well before any single card. The board fills with permanents whose purpose is unclear, every card carries a paragraph of text, and with thousands of legal commanders there is a good chance you are looking at cards you have never played against. A newer player might be tracking ten times as many unfamiliar cards as they would in a sixty-card duel. The Avengers precon is the answer to that problem inside its own box. It is the clean go-wide counters deck, the one a table can follow at a glance, and the best upgrades keep it that way.

#### Two kinds of overload  
*The core idea*

I split the information problem in two. The first is card diversity: a hundred-card singleton format with a huge legal pool means every deck is packed with cards you may never have seen, and you are reading three of those decks at once. The second is gameplay diversity: when every deck also does something rare and specific, you cannot guess anyone's plan from a few early cards. Put both together at a high-power casual table and threat assessment turns into a coin flip, because you have no honest read on who is about to win.

> The Avengers plan reads itself. Heroes hit the board, Captain America hands out haste and a counter, the team gets bigger. No hidden lock, no combo assembled from three unrelated cards.

This deck belongs on the easy side of both problems. Its cards are Heroes and its plan is to go wide with +1/+1 counters, so an opponent can see the whole game coming and plan around it. Captain America, Team Leader gives every other Hero vigilance, haste, and a counter as it enters. Director Nick Fury makes those Heroes cheaper and digs for more when you attack. A table can watch that build turn by turn and never lose the thread. My point is that this legibility is a real feature, and it is worth protecting when you upgrade.

#### Say the plan out loud  
*Table manners*

Before the cards even matter, here is a free fix I lean on: tell the table what your deck does. I explain my own deck as I shuffle and ask opponents for the same, and I flag my dangerous spells by name, saying I am attempting to cast the game-ender instead of mumbling it out. That habit treats opponents like seasoned players who deserve a fair chance to respond. The Avengers deck is the ideal deck to run this way, because the honest one-line pitch, go wide and pile on counters with Captain America, is the whole truth. There is no gotcha to hide, so leaning into the openness costs you nothing and makes the game better for everyone.

#### Trade a narrow trigger for a legible one  
*Swap 1*


**Love on the Battlefield → Champions from Beyond**
Love on the Battlefield triggers only when you attack with exactly two creatures, which cuts against a go-wide deck's instinct to swing with the team, so it often does nothing. Champions from Beyond makes Hero tokens, a payoff the whole table can read, and each token that enters visibly triggers Captain America's counter anthem. The plan you are running shows on the board where opponents can see it coming, which is the readable line this deck wants.

#### A wipe that keeps one clear board  
*Swap 2*


**Avenge → Damning Verdict**
My favorite pool of decks stayed readable because it was interaction dense and nobody overcommitted to the board. Damning Verdict carries that idea here. It wipes creatures without counters and usually spares most of your counter-loaded team, so one messy board resets into a mostly clean one you still control. Captain America himself can be caught if no other Hero has entered to hand him a counter, so get a body down first. That is the opposite of the noise I warn about, where removal is absent and every table ends up buried under a pile of synergy permanents nobody can parse.

#### One plain effect beats a fiddly loop  
*Swap 3*


**Gift of Immortality → Wave Goodbye**
Gift of Immortality is a recurring protection loop, the kind of effect the whole table has to remember every time a creature dies. Wave Goodbye is a single board bounce that does one clear thing when you cast it. Trading the loop for the one-shot removes a standing rule from everyone's mental load and gives the deck a straightforward answer. It is a real cost, since Gift of Immortality gave a key creature genuine recursion, so make the swap for legibility and know what you are giving up. Cleaner to pilot, cleaner to play against.

#### Stronger and still readable  
*The through-line*

Every swap here trades a fiddly or narrow effect for a clean one, so the deck gets more consistent while staying easy to follow. That is the exact profile a newer pilot wants at a pickup table, and it is the profile that makes the game better for the three people sitting across from them. I love weird, unusual decks, and I am not telling you to stop building them. I am asking you to notice that a readable deck is a kindness at a table full of strangers, and the Avengers precon is already built to be that deck. Keep it that way and it holds its ground while asking far less of everyone's attention.

> A deck the whole table can read is a gift to the whole table. The Avengers box hands you one already; the upgrades only have to keep it clear.

## Wakanda Forever
*Selesnya (G/W) · from my video: When Good Cards Are the Wrong Choice (YouTube `owPWRYhY1VA`)*

### When a Good Card Is the Wrong Choice

*The most single-minded of the four, built around one Vibranium-and-counters engine, has an identity worth protecting. Some powerful cards fight it. Here is how to tell, worked the way I work mine.*

Here is a question I like to open with. Say a card is powerful, meshes with how a deck works, and shores up a real weak spot. When is putting it in still the wrong call? Casual Commander gives that question teeth, because a casual deck is one you have chosen to keep short of fully optimal on purpose. There are always stronger cards you could run. So the interesting cases are the good cards that quietly cost you the thing you actually enjoy, or that work against what the deck is really about. The Wakanda precon is the most single-minded of the four, built around T'Challa spinning up indestructible Vibranium tokens and growing on big artifact spells, and a few strong cards pull it away from that.

#### The cost that lives off the stat line  
*The core idea*

My examples all share a shape. A card looks like a slam dunk, then it shortcuts past the part of the deck that made it fun, or it drags a hidden cost the card text never mentions. Monument to Endurance turned my cycling deck's rich tutoring puzzle into a plain do-a-thing-get-a-card engine. Thousand-Year Storm erased value and mana as concepts in a deck that was supposed to grind out two-for-ones. Both cards read as pure upgrades and both quietly flattened the deck. The lesson carries straight onto Wakanda. Judge each card by its stat line and you keep every strong include. Judge it by what it does to T'Challa's Vibranium and counters plan, and by what it costs you at the table, and a few of them stop looking so obvious.

> Optimizing is fine. The move is to optimize for the Vibranium and counters identity, not for raw power that ignores it.


#### The card whose real price is the target it paints  
*Swap 1, the headline*


**Coveted Jewel → Batterskull**
Coveted Jewel is the clearest case of the whole idea. By raw rate it is excellent: it draws three on cast, taps for three mana, and even triggers T'Challa. The case against it has nothing to do with synergy. Its real price stays off the stat line, since whoever hits you unblocked steals it and draws three, which hands an opponent your card advantage and gives the whole table a reason to point attacks your way. In a grindy Selesnya deck that wants to build its own board on its own clock, that is a liability the text never spells out. Batterskull does the honest version of the job, a recurring lifelink threat that protects you and closes games without begging the table to swing at you.

#### Serve the counters plan, not a generic engine  
*Swap 2*


**Sword of the Animist → Verdurous Gearhulk**
Sword of the Animist is a two-mana artifact that fetches a land whenever the equipped creature attacks, so it is on-theme, only slow and small. At two mana it never reaches T'Challa's four-or-greater counter trigger, and the equip cost competes with better uses of your mana. Verdurous Gearhulk arrives with four +1/+1 counters and pushes the exact plan T'Challa and Shuri reward. Play it for that counters payoff. Better still, it is a five-mana artifact creature, so casting it also triggers T'Challa for two more counters. You get the payoff twice, once from its own four counters and once from the commander. This is the other half of my point in practice: pick the tool that deepens the deck's real game instead of a generically fine card that does nothing for it.

#### Cheap protection for the plan you care about  
*Swap 3*


**Gilded Lotus → Reprieve**
Gilded Lotus is not off-plan the way it first looks: as a five-mana artifact it triggers T'Challa for two counters and taps for three mana of any color. The real knock is tempo, since five mana that only makes more mana is slow for where this deck wants to be by then. Reprieve trades that slow rock for cheap insurance, a two-mana instant that returns a removal spell to its owner's hand and replaces itself. It answers spells, not abilities or combat, so treat it as targeted cover for T'Challa. I keep recommending the less flashy tools that serve a deck's purpose, and a cheap protection spell earns its slot here far better than another big colorless rock.

#### Optimize for the vibe, then chase it  
*The through-line*

I will be honest that putting weaker-looking cards in a deck runs against every deckbuilding instinct, and I am a self-described optimizer myself. My fix is to keep optimizing, only for something other than raw power. For Wakanda that something is the Vibranium and counters identity that is the whole reason to buy the box. Cut the strong card that paints a target, skip the generic engine that ignores the plan, and fill those slots with cards that push the counters and guard the pieces. A win with this deck feels earned when it comes from its own hardware, so give it the tools that make the Vibranium engine the star and let the flashier options sit in someone else's deck.

> The headline call is Coveted Jewel, a strong card whose real cost is the target it paints on you. The rest tighten the deck around the plan a T'Challa build wants to play.
