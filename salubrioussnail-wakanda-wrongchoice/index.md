# Wakanda: When a Good Card Is the Wrong Choice

The most focused deck in the Marvel Super Heroes Commander box has an identity worth protecting, and a few strong cards fight it. This is Salubrious Snail's when-good-cards-are-the-wrong-choice video worked on the actual precon, instead of a link to it.

## The cost that lives off the stat line

My examples share a shape: a card looks like a slam dunk, then it shortcuts past the fun part of the deck or drags a hidden cost the text never mentions. Monument to Endurance flattened my cycling deck's tutoring puzzle into a plain do-a-thing-get-a-card engine; Thousand-Year Storm erased value and mana as concepts in a grindy two-for-one deck. Judged by stat line, both read as pure upgrades. Judged by what they do to the deck's real game, both had to go. For Wakanda the test is the same: does a card help T'Challa's Vibranium and counters plan, or does it add generic power that pulls the deck off its own game.

## The three swaps

- **Coveted Jewel → Batterskull (the headline).** Coveted Jewel is excellent by raw rate and even triggers T'Challa, so the case against it has nothing to do with synergy. Its real price stays off the stat line: whoever hits you unblocked steals it and draws three. Batterskull does the honest version, a recurring lifelink threat that protects and closes without painting a target.
- **Sword of the Animist → Verdurous Gearhulk.** A small two-mana artifact (too cheap to trigger T'Challa) for a five-mana artifact creature that arrives with four +1/+1 counters and, because it is an artifact, triggers T'Challa for two more. The counters payoff comes twice over.
- **Gilded Lotus → Reprieve.** Gilded Lotus does trigger T'Challa and makes colored mana, so the cut is tempo, since five mana that only makes mana is slow. Reprieve is cheap insurance that returns a removal spell to its owner's hand and cantrips; it answers spells, not abilities or combat.

## Optimize for the vibe, then chase it

I am a self-described optimizer and admit that running weaker-looking cards fights every instinct. My fix is to keep optimizing, only for something other than raw power. For Wakanda that something is the Vibranium and counters identity that is the whole reason to buy the box. Cut the strong card that paints a target, skip the generic engine that ignores the plan, and fill those slots with cards that push counters and guard the pieces. A win from the deck's own hardware feels earned.
