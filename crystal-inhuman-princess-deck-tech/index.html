Crystal, Inhuman Princess: The Four‑Color Ping

The most-built Fantastic Four commander is none of the four faces. Crystal pings each opponent every time you cast a noncreature spell, for the number of colors that spell is. Cast colored spells, make them multicolor, and the whole table bleeds.

Crystal, Inhuman Princess costs {1}{R}{G}, and her color identity is all four of white, blue, red, and green, earned through her mana ability rather than her casting cost. She flies, she taps for {R}, {G}, {W}, or {U}, and her engine reads: whenever you cast a noncreature spell, she deals X damage to each opponent, where X is the number of colors that spell is. She is the Fantastic Four spellslinger, and EDHREC records her as the most-built commander in the box, above every advertised face. The whole deck is two dials, how many noncreature spells you cast, and how many colors each one carries.

Crystal costs three mana and can tap for four different colors. She flies. Her main power: each noncreature spell you cast hits every opponent for the number of colors in that spell. A two-color spell hits for two, a five-color spell hits for five. She is the most popular Fantastic Four commander.

The engine, two dials

Crystal turns your spellcasting into direct damage on a formula anyone can read. Cast a noncreature spell of X colors, deal X to each opponent. In a four-player pod that is X to three opponents at once, so a single two-color instant is six damage spread across the table. The deck wins by casting many colored spells and by pushing the average color count of those spells upward. Her flying keeps a two-power clock in the air on top of the pings, and her mana ability fixes the four-color base she wants.

This makes Crystal a spellslinger with a scaling reward that most spellslingers lack. A Storm-style commander cares only about spell count. Crystal cares about spell count and color count together, which changes the deckbuilding question from how many noncreature spells can I cast to how many colored pips can I cast per spell. The two dials multiply.

Her damage is simple: colors of the spell, to each opponent. With three opponents, a two-color spell deals six total. So you want to cast lots of noncreature spells, and you want each spell to have as many colors as possible.

The color-count lever

X scales straight with the color count of the spell, so a Crystal deck lives or fails on how colorful its noncreature spells are. A mono-color spell pings one per opponent. A gold two-color spell doubles that. A three-color spell triples it. The lever is the average color count across the noncreature package, and it is the number the build should track most closely.

Colorless noncreature spell (most rocks, plain artifacts)0 each
Mono-color spell1 each
Two-color spell2 each
Three-color spell3 each
Five-color spell5 each

Read the table down the right column, then multiply by three for the pod. A three-color spell is nine damage across the table off one cast. The takeaway for construction is direct: every point of average color count you add to the noncreature suite is worth three damage per spell in a four-player game. Multicolor removal, multicolor card draw, and multicolor value spells are all better here than their mono-color equivalents, even at a slightly higher mana cost.

One color equals one damage each, two colors equals two, and so on. Then times three for the whole table. So a three-color spell is nine damage to the table from one cast. Every extra color on your spells is worth a lot.

Colorless is zero, the build's first rule

The formula counts colors, and a colorless spell is zero colors, so it pings for nothing. That single fact reshapes the ninety-nine. Most mana rocks, most plain artifacts, and any colorless utility spell deal zero Crystal damage, so the usual precon reflex of loading up on artifact ramp works against her. Fix the mana with lands and with colored ramp where the color pips still count, and treat every colorless noncreature spell as a card that has to justify its slot on its own, since it adds nothing to the drain.

This is Crystal's version of the reveal-is-not-draw trap. A build can look busy, cast a noncreature spell most turns, and still drain almost nothing if half those spells are colorless rocks. Count the colored noncreature spells separately from the colorless ones, and keep the colorless group small and purposeful.

Colorless spells count as zero colors, so they deal zero damage with Crystal. That means most mana rocks and plain artifacts do nothing for her. Use lands and colored ramp instead, and keep colorless spells to a minimum.

Noncreature spell density, the second dial

The other dial is how many noncreature spells you cast across a game, because each one is a separate trigger. Crystal is grindy math rather than a burst kill. To drain one opponent from forty on Crystal pings alone takes a stack of casts, and the average color count sets how tall that stack is. At an average of two colors it takes twenty noncreature spells to finish one opponent, and at three colors it takes about thirteen. Both numbers come down fast once flying damage, other Fantastic Four payoffs, and combat join the pings.

The build therefore wants a high count of cheap colored noncreature spells and every way to cast more of them per turn: cost reducers, flashback and rebound, cantrips that replace themselves, and copy effects that double the color-count trigger. A copy of a three-color spell is another nine to the table. This is where the spellslinger toolkit and Crystal's formula reinforce each other.

Each noncreature spell is one trigger, so you want to cast a lot of them. Crystal drains slowly, so add cost reducers, cheap spells that replace themselves, and copy effects. Copying a spell copies the damage too.

Beyond the Dead Draw Audit: the construction audit

The Dead Draw Audit scores single cards. This construction audit scores the whole ninety-nine on Crystal's two dials, noncreature spell count and average color count. The first rule is a floor on colored noncreature spells, the second is a target average color count, and the third is a cap on colorless spells that do nothing for the drain.

Card advantage needs its own read. Crystal turns spells into damage, and a drain deck still needs to keep casting, so the colored cantrips and multicolor draw spells do double duty as fuel and as pings. Report two numbers when you tune the list, the colored noncreature count and the average color count across that group. A build that reports a high spell count while half those spells are colorless is overstating its clock. The honest measure is total expected color-count damage per turn, spells times average colors times three opponents.

Every revision should hold the colored-spell floor first. A new colorless card should replace another colorless card so the drain does not thin. A new mono-color spell should give way to a multicolor spell of the same effect when one exists, since the extra pip is three damage a cast. Extra-cast enablers and multicolor payoffs are the two packages that keep the formula compounding, and neither should be cut to add another plain rock.

Crystal is two dials multiplied: how many colored spells you cast, and how many colors each one carries.

To build Crystal: pack in colored noncreature spells, aim for 34 to 40, and make at least half of them multicolor. Keep colorless spells low because they deal zero. Add cost reducers and copy effects so you cast more each turn. Track two numbers: how many colored spells, and how many colors on average.

The shell, the Fantastic Four spellslinger base

Crystal is part of the unchanged Fantastic Four ninety-nine and shares the four-color spellslinger base laid out on the Mister Fantastic deck tech and the Fantastic Four manabase page, so the full list and the mana work live there. Under Crystal the shell tilts three ways. The noncreature spells shift toward gold and three-color cards wherever a multicolor version of an effect exists, the colorless rock package shrinks in favor of colored ramp and a slightly higher land count, and the payoff slots favor copy effects and cost reducers that let you cast more colored spells per turn.

The interaction, the card draw, and the win conditions carry over, since a spellslinger wants the same colored instants and sorceries whether the face is Mister Fantastic or Crystal. Mister Fantastic doubles a noncreature trigger for a turn, and Crystal makes every noncreature spell a table-wide ping, so the two commanders reward the same core and split only on the payoff shell. Build toward the construction audit above, a high colored-spell count, a multicolor majority, and a short colorless list.
