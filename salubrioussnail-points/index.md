## #1. Using Math to Decide How Many Bad Cards to Run  (Jun 2026, 27K views)
*Original title: It's okay to put *some* janky trash in your deck*
**Category:** Deckbuilding
**Tags:** Pet Cards, Deck Consistency, Card Evaluation, Deckbuilding Principle

We argue that Commander decks can include some bad or situational cards without harming consistency if they can reliably reach a post-engine state with high card draw.
We recommend using hypergeometric calculations to check the odds of drawing too many early-game dead cards.
By categorizing cards into premium enablers, situational blanks, and general support, deckbuilders can balance fun inclusions with a functional early game.

**Principle**
- Commander decks often have a pre-engine phase with limited card access and a post-engine phase where drawing extra cards makes individual card quality less important.
- Including some situational or weak cards can be acceptable if the deck can reliably reach the post-engine state.

**Evidence**
- Hypergeometric probabilities show that with about five situational blanks, the chance of drawing multiple such cards early is low.
- Fast lands entering untapped early demonstrate how situational cards can be reliable when later card selection becomes available.

**Deckbuilding Application**
- Categorize nonland cards into three buckets: high-quality enablers, situational blanks, and general support to assess early-game consistency.
- Use a hypergeometric calculator to check that the probability of drawing an engine starter is high and the probability of drawing multiple blanks is low.

**Limitation**
- If the number of bucket two cards grows into double digits, the risk of drawing too many dead early cards rises and can disrupt deck function.

**Example**
- A Radha, Heir to Keld deck runs a single humorous seven-drop like Tyrant of Discord as a bucket two card that does not harm early consistency.
- Protection spells like Heroic Intervention are dead early but valuable later, exemplifying how situational cards can be included sparingly.

**More quotes**
> It might actually be okay to have some bad cards in your deck.
> You can check how often you won't draw cards you don't want.
> I think of a lot of Commander decks as having two very distinct phases, pre-engine and post-engine.
> Getting to purge the trash for the first time in a game and sculpt a hand of seven beautiful, situationally applicable cards, delicious.
> You can almost think about all of these bucket two bad cards as blank for the purposes of your early game.
> Craft the foundation of your deck in a way that's maximally consistent and flexible, and the amount of room to sprinkle garbage into that bad boy might just surprise you.
> My Radha Heir to Keld deck has a single permanent funny seven drop deck slot. It was occupied by Panglacial Wurm in the past, and it's currently occupied by Tyrant of Discord, both not very good creatures.
> Treat this as your reward for being such a responsible deck builder in other ways.
> The first three turns of the game are the ones where you're going to be the hardest pressed to have a land that both enters untapped and gives the colors you need, and fast lands fill this exact profile.
> With any other commander, this deck's ratios would make no sense at all and it would flail around doing nothing most of the time.

**Principle**

**Deckbuilding Application**
- Choose a commander that independently synergizes with the secret commander to reduce card slot demands.
- Ensure support cards advance your visible commander's strategy to avoid dead draws.

**Limitation**
- If a hidden commander demands heavy support without analogs, the deck becomes vulnerable to targeted removal.

**Example**
- The Sibsig Ceremony deck used Athreos and other indestructible creatures to turn forced sacrifice into token generation.

**More quotes**

## #3. The Problem With Magic's One-Off Set Structure  (May 2026, 135K views)
*Original title: The Subtle Reason Why Sets Feel Worse Now*
**Category:** Meta Commentary
**Tags:** Format Health, Design Intent, Meta Trends

We argue that Magic's shift from multi-set blocks to yearly one-off sets has made the game feel rushed and disconnected.
It acknowledges that three-set blocks often had filler sets that didn't convey the story well.
We propose that an alternative set structure could better balance immersion and mechanical iteration.

**Principle**
- Multi-set blocks enable players to become immersed in a plane's setting and storyline over time.
- Mechanical themes develop more fully when given multiple sets to iterate and expand.

**Evidence**
- No new plane has had a multi-set block since Ixalan, which concluded over 8 years ago.
- Later sets within a block historically sold worse than the first set.
- Currently, Magic releases five or more one-off sets per year, each on a different plane.

**Deckbuilding Application**
- Deckbuilders should evaluate new mechanics for their standalone power, as one-off sets rarely provide follow-up support.
- Without mechanical iteration across sets, synergy-based archetypes become riskier to build around.

**Limitation**
- Three-set blocks sometimes forced players to experience a poorly received plane for an entire year.
- Crafting satisfying limited environments across three connected sets was difficult for developers.

**Example**
- Dragon's Maze, the third set of Return to Ravnica block, attempted to feature all 10 guilds but felt cramped and failed to convey its maze race story.

**More quotes**
> Having every set be a new plane, particularly with five or more sets per year, makes things feel rushed, leaving no time to breathe and become immersed in a particular world.
> The gradual pivot from three-set blocks to purely one-off sets has been an unambiguous tragedy.
> I could tell you that we as magic players are fallen and need to return to the glory days of 2013, where magic sets came in threes and where bold duels could take place on a middle school lunch table.
> Dragon's Maze simply didn't feel like a proper third set, rather just a little bit of extra Ravnica with some attached plot that not enough effort was put into guiding us through with its design.
> Born of the Gods felt more like Theros B-sides than anything else, just some kind of odd designs to go along with a group of five gods in order to allow the 15 gods to be evenly spread between the three sets.
> Fate Reforged feels more like the middle of an Animorphs cover than it does its own cohesive world.
> It's okay to have a hat set. However, for that sort of set to feel justified, it has to feel differentiated in some way from what surrounds.
> The biggest reason I like two-set blocks is that they're able to tell the story of a place much better than a one-set block could.
> Seeing that clear change communicated through the design of a set would be great for getting players to engage more with the lore and ask more questions, as opposed to one-off sets that present a snapshot of things and then dash away.
> One-off sets can be great as a way to do something funky and unusual, to explore some bold new aesthetic territory without committing too hard.

## #4. How to Integrate Subthemes into Your Commander Deck  (May 2026, 48K views)

**Category:** Deckbuilding
**Tags:** Blink, Tokens, Discard, Synergy, Card Evaluation, Deck Consistency, Deckbuilding Principle

We explain how to use fulcrum cards to connect subthemes in Commander decks.
We emphasize evaluating a card's worst-case performance, or floor, and the deck space it requires.
By assessing these factors, builders can avoid dead draws and create more cohesive multi-theme decks.

**Principle**
- The floor of a card, how it performs in a suboptimal scenario, is the key metric for including subtheme enablers.

**Evidence**
- Cards like Professional Facebreaker have high floors by providing generic value, while cards like Astral Slide are dead without cycling.

**Deckbuilding Application**
- To avoid dead draws, assess if a card's floor is acceptable by imagining the 25th percentile game.
- When adding a subtheme, calculate the deck space required; cards that demand heavy support may not be worth the slots.

**Limitation**
- Even synergistic-looking cards can fail if their floor is too low for the deck's context, as seen with Ret to my mystic in a cycling-heavy deck.

**Example**
- Squee, Dubious Monarch exemplifies a fulcrum card by converting graveyard setup into token generation, bridging two themes.

**More quotes**
> Subthemes can make your deck better or they can make your deck worse. Either might be a desirable outcome depending on what you look for in a game of Magic: The Gathering.
> Having your deck do more stuff, have more textured play paths, and produce more unexpected results is desirable for players who have already played a lot with more linear styles of deck.
> A good place to start in expanding your deck's synergy universe is what I'll call fulcrum cards: things that naturally tie together multiple subthemes.
> An important consideration when thinking about [fulcrum] cards is the floor of the effect. I'm stat brained, so I think about the 25th percentile game: how will the card feel when it's not living up to our wildest dreams?
> It's important to figure out what works as a minor part of a deck and what has no business being outside a deck that doesn't heavily support it due to its low floor.
> [Ret to the Mystic], a little bird, gets mugged by a merfolk looter in this particular deck.
> Sometimes you're just going to have a bit of a hard time combining two themes. Does this mean that you shouldn't smash together two very different synergy families? No.
> Artifacts plus ETB and tokens plus one/one counters have been done tens of thousands of times before for good reason, but you always have the opportunity to do something different, unique, and more specifically suited to who you are as a player.
> Card selection tools like filtering spells and conditional tutors will reduce the play pattern variance of a deck, but if you add them to a deck that would otherwise have extreme variance due to many moving parts, you'll likely end up with something still very fresh and dynamic to play.
> Is Spread the Sickness the best removal this game has to offer? Definitely not, but it feels immensely at home in this deck and I wouldn't dare cut.

## #6. How to Avoid Common Commander Deckbuilding Mistakes  (Jan 2026, 259K views)

**Category:** Deckbuilding
**Tags:** Deck Consistency, Resilience, Win Conditions, Synergy, Deckbuilding Principle

We analyze recurring deckbuilding mistakes observed in two years of deck reviews.
It identifies unfocused game plans, vulnerability to disruption, and lack of proactivity as common issues.
We advocate for tighter synergy focus, versatile protection over narrow spells, and ramp or haste to increase consistency.
These lessons aim to help players refine their Commander decks into more cohesive and resilient strategies.

**Principle**
- A deck should have a clear primary game plan; mixing multiple payoff directions weakens the deck's focus.
- Restraint in card inclusion is crucial; even cards with thematic synergy can dilute a strategy if they do not support the core win condition.

**Evidence**
- Unfocused macro goals were found in seven reviewed decks, making it a common issue especially among newer players.

**Deckbuilding Application**
- When selecting payoffs for a synergy like landfall, choose ones that all contribute to a unified strategy (such as tokens and buffs) and avoid adding cards that generate incremental life gain or damage if they do not align.
- To protect a fragile game plan, use versatile tools like counterspells, haste enablers, or ramp instead of narrow protection spells that are dead when behind.

**Limitation**
- Protection spells can be ineffective when you are behind on board, making them a risky inclusion in decks that need to catch up.
- Relying solely on expensive commanders or resource-intensive setups increases vulnerability to disruption.

**Example**
- A landfall deck aiming to win via token beatdown should avoid including cards like Grazing Gladeheart (life gain) or Tunneling Geopede (incremental damage) because they dilute the primary aggressive plan.
- Using haste sources like Anger or end-step enablers like Leyline of Anticipation can effectively protect key creatures without dedicated protection spells.

**More quotes**

> A necessary lesson here is in restraint, knowing when to exclude a card, even when it theoretically has a bit of synergy with what you're building.
> The worst feeling is having a creature eat dirt before it could do anything at all.
> If you're adding a goad card to your deck, its effectiveness will be inversely proportional to the number of good cards already in your deck.
> [If you overload on goad], pretend cards are fogs instead and suddenly your deck with 12 fogs in it is looking a little suspect.
> Some decks are a bit of a glass cannon. They have the potential to be very powerful, but can easily get kneecapped and do nothing.
> If you're cruising through Scryfall with a certain search term related to your synergy family, it's easy to just add endlessly until your deck space has filled up.
> I don't like protection spells very much. I've had multiple protection heavy decks that just felt kind of bad to play.

**Category:** Deckbuilding
**Tags:** Ramp, Deck Consistency, Mana Base, Curve, Synergy, Deckbuilding Principle

Ramp serves two distinct functions: smoothing out the early curve and increasing total mana over the game.
We provide a framework to decide if a deck needs more ramp based on its curve, commander cost, and game plan.
Hypergeometric calculators can quantify the right amount, with roughly 18 ramp spells giving a high chance of curving out.

**Principle**
- Ramp spells perform two separate functions: curving out and increasing total mana quantity.
- Deciding on ramp requires considering the mana values of other cards and the desired pacing of plays.

**Evidence**
- In deck reviews, more decks needed additional ramp than additional card draw.

**Deckbuilding Application**
- Use a hypergeometric calculator to match the number of ramp spells to your desired chance of curving out.
- Aim for around 18 ramp spells for a 77% chance of having one by turn three.
- Choose ramp cost that aligns with your curve gaps, such as two-cost ramp if you lack two-drops but have many four-drops.

**Limitation**
- Adding ramp without purpose can dilute deck synergy and make it less powerful.
- Mana dorks are efficient for curve but fragile against board wipes; prioritize land ramp for long-game mana growth.

**Example**
- A dinosaur deck wanting to cast its four-cost commander on turn four can ramp on turn two to have four mana on turn three and then cast it on schedule.

**More quotes**
> Adding ramp feels like a somewhat dated suggestion, a tip offered to newer players in 2018.
> In 2025, card draw and synergy feel like bigger focal points for EDH critique, with a trendy hot take being that people run too much ramp.
> Ramp spells don't fulfill one single task. Rather, they perform two separate functions: curving out and increasing total mana quantity.
> Adding ramp to a deck without thinking about why you're adding it is both a commonplace action and a recipe for decks that aren't as lean and powerful as they could be.
> Ramping on turn one is theoretically the most mana efficient, but basically all one mana ramp will be in the form of mana dorks, which could amplify the weakness to board wipes.
> An Elvish Mystic will be great for a buttery smooth curve out, but an EDH game is a hostile climate for a squishy guy like that in the long term.
> A deck with a high 30s land count is naturally going to start missing land drops around turn six absent any draw or filtering.
> If you're just getting started with a new deck, a good starting point will be to aim high with both draw and ramp.
> [My] Malfagore deck runs very little ramp. The goal is to push all players, myself included, into a low resources state.
> Consistent land drops are really all that's needed to keep up with [my commander's] command tax.

**Principle**

**Limitation**
- Even when including protection for the commander, decks like Kaalia remain vulnerable to board wipes because they rely on a fragile attacker and a slow creature-based strategy.

**Example**
