Director Nick Fury: The Hero‑Value Engine

The Avengers box's second commander is the grinder rather than the closer. Nick Fury drops the cost of every Hero you cast and turns each attack into a dig for another Hero. Build the Hero count high and both abilities pay you every turn.
Nick Fury is the patient Avengers commander. He makes your Hero cards cheaper, and every time you attack he digs for another Hero. The more Heroes in your deck, the better both powers work.

Director Nick Fury costs {U}{R}{W} and runs on two abilities that both scale with one number, the count of Heroes in your deck. Hero spells you cast cost one less. And whenever you attack, you look at the top four cards of your library, reveal a Hero from among them, and put it into your hand. He is Jeskai, he is a 2/4 Human Spy Hero, and he leads the same Avengers Assemble shell Captain America leads. The difference is the plan. Captain America builds a wide counter board for one explosive turn. Nick Fury grinds, cheaper Heroes every turn and a fresh Hero in hand on every swing, until the value buries the table.

Nick Fury has two powers. Your Hero cards cost one less mana, and every time you attack you look at the top four cards and take a Hero. Both powers get stronger when your deck is packed with Heroes. He wins slowly by out-carding everyone instead of taking one big turn.

The engine, two abilities on one axis

The whole deck turns on Hero density. The first ability, Hero spells cost one less, is ramp measured in Heroes: every Hero you cast refunds a generic mana, so two Heroes a turn is two mana of acceleration that no removal can strip off a rock. The second ability, the attack dig, is card advantage measured in Heroes: four cards deep, take the Hero. A deck thin on Heroes gets a cost cut that rarely fires and a dig that often whiffs. A deck thick with Heroes gets a discount most turns and a near-automatic card off every attack.

Read the dig carefully. It reveals a Hero into your hand, and revealing is not drawing. Cards that reward you for drawing, and payoffs that count on a draw trigger, do not fire off Nick Fury's dig, off Avengers Tower, or off Herald's Horn. That single line separates a Nick Fury build from a generic Jeskai draw deck, and it steers the payoff choices later on this page.

Both of Nick Fury's powers work better with more Heroes: the discount fires more often and the attack dig almost always finds one. One catch: his dig reveals a Hero, and revealing is not the same as drawing, so cards that say draw do not get triggered by it.

Hero-density math: the dig hit-rate

Nick Fury looks four cards deep and takes a Hero. Whether that attack draws a card depends entirely on how many Heroes sit in the ninety-nine. At the Avengers shell's 27 Heroes, an attack reveals at least one Hero about 72.7 percent of the time. Push the count up and the dig approaches automatic. This is the single most useful number for building the deck, and it argues for a higher Hero count than a Captain America list needs.

22 Heroes in the 99, one attack finds a Hero64.1%
27 Heroes (the Avengers shell), one attack finds a Hero72.7%
32 Heroes, one attack finds a Hero79.6%
37 Heroes, one attack finds a Hero85.2%

Two attackers a turn double the card flow, so a developed board with two swings and 32 Heroes expects better than one and a half Hero cards a turn from Nick Fury alone. That is the grind. It also means the deck wants attackers with reach or evasion, since a Hero that cannot connect provides a body but no dig. Nick Fury himself is a 2/4, a fine early attacker into an open board and a poor one once blockers arrive, so his own trigger benefits from a way through.

With 27 Heroes, Nick Fury finds a Hero on about 73 out of 100 attacks. With 37 Heroes it is 85. So the more Heroes you run, the more his attack draws you a card. And you want creatures that can actually get through to attack, or the dig never happens.

Cost reduction as Hero-shaped ramp

Hero spells cost one less. On a curve where most Heroes cost two to four, one generic off each is a large discount, and it compounds when Nick Fury's dig keeps handing you more Heroes to cast. Two Heroes in a turn is two mana back, which is the difference between one play and two. The discount does not touch your removal, your rocks, or your card draw, so the build should keep the Hero count high enough that the reduction is doing work on most turns rather than sitting idle.

The cost cut also changes how the mana base and the rock package should be sized. A Captain America list ramps hard to reach an explosive turn. A Nick Fury list can run a slightly leaner rock package, because the commander itself is a repeatable discount that behaves like ramp once the Hero count is high. Spend the freed slots on more Heroes and more ways to connect in combat.

Every Hero costs one less mana with Nick Fury out. Cast two Heroes and you save two mana, which is often a whole extra play. Because he is like built-in ramp, you can run a few fewer mana rocks and more Heroes instead.

Beyond the Dead Draw Audit: the construction audit

The Dead Draw Audit scores single cards. This construction audit scores the whole ninety-nine, and for Nick Fury the governing number is Hero density. Both of his abilities pay out in proportion to the Hero count, so the first construction rule is a Hero floor. A Captain America list is happy at 27 Heroes because his counters come from entries. A Nick Fury list should climb toward the low thirties, because every added Hero raises the dig hit-rate and widens the discount at the same time.

Ramp and rocks6-8Leaner than a Cap list, since the commander is a repeatable discount

Card advantage needs a second read under this commander. Nick Fury supplies selection rather than raw cards, since his dig hands you one Hero and buries the other three. That keeps the hand stocked with threats and does nothing for a payoff that wants a draw trigger. So the ninety-nine should carry a small package of genuine draw spells, Kindred Discovery and a few others, kept separate in the count from Nick Fury's selection. A build that reports its Nick Fury digs as if they were draws is overstating its card advantage. List selection and draw as two numbers.

Every revision should hold the Hero floor first. A new noncreature card should replace another noncreature card so the Hero count does not slip. A new Hero should replace a weaker Hero or a spell the deck can spare. Combat access and real draw are the two support packages that keep the engine honest, and neither should be cut to add a fourth anthem. The finished list should attack most turns, find a Hero most attacks, and cast that Hero at a discount.

Both of Nick Fury's abilities pay out in Heroes, so the deck is built one number at a time: raise the Hero count.

The big rule for building Nick Fury is: pack in Heroes, aim for about 30 to 34. His dig gives you selection instead of extra draws, so also run a few real draw cards like Kindred Discovery, and count them separately. Keep some ways to get through in combat so the attack dig always happens.

Cap or Fury: the two faces of the shell

Avengers Assemble ships two full-identity commanders for the unchanged deck, and both are Jeskai, so the color identity and most of the ninety-nine stay the same. Captain America, Team Leader turns every Hero entry into counters and haste, a go-wide board that closes in one modified turn. Director Nick Fury leads the grindier build, cheaper Heroes and a steady dig that favors card advantage and a Legends-and-value plan over one explosive counter turn. Move Nick Fury to the command zone when you would rather out-grind the table than race it.

The Avengers box has two commanders for this deck. Captain America is fast and explosive with counters. Nick Fury is slow and steady with cheaper Heroes and card selection. Pick Fury when you want the long grind instead of one big turn.

The shell, and how it shifts under Fury

Nick Fury leads the same grounded Jeskai shell laid out card by card on the Captain America deck tech, so the full hundred lives there rather than repeated here. The Hero package is the spine: Black Widow, Ant-Man, Silver Sable, Hawkeye, Spider-Woman, The Wasp, Falcon and Redwing, Shang-Chi, Scarlet Witch, Iron Man, Photon, Vision, Winter Soldier, War Machine, and the rest, most costing two to four. Under Nick Fury the list makes three shifts. Captain America moves from the command zone into the ninety-nine as one more Hero, Nick Fury takes his place, and the counters-only payoffs give way to more Heroes, more real draw, and a couple of unblockable enablers so the dig fires every turn.

The manabase, the rocks, and the interaction carry over intact, since a grind deck wants the same answers a go-wide deck does. The build target is the construction audit above: thirty or more Heroes, a small true-draw package kept separate in the count, and enough combat access that Nick Fury connects. That is the whole difference between the two faces, one number raised and the payoffs re-pointed from counters to cards.

Nick Fury uses the same deck as the Captain America page, so the full list is over there. The changes: put Captain America into the deck as a Hero, make Nick Fury the commander, and swap some counter cards for more Heroes, real draw, and ways to get through in combat.
